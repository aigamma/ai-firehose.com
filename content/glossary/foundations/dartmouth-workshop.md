The founding document of artificial intelligence contains one of the great underestimates in the history of science. In 1956 a small group proposed to spend the summer at Dartmouth College attacking the problem of making machines think, on the conjecture "that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." They expected meaningful progress in a couple of months. Seventy years later the project is still running. The Dartmouth Summer Research Project is conventionally treated as the birth of AI as a field, and its proposal is where the term itself was first put into print.

The name was a deliberate act of territory-marking. John McCarthy, then a young mathematician at Dartmouth, chose "artificial intelligence" in part to stake out ground distinct from the established but narrower banner of cybernetics and from the specific commitments of those already working on neural nets and automata. He organized the meeting with Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester of IBM, and Claude Shannon of Bell Labs. The phrase was almost a provocation, an insistence that thinking itself, not merely control or communication, was the object of study, and it stuck so completely that the older, more biological tradition it displaced is now largely forgotten.

The workshop matters less for anything it produced than for what it consolidated, because it produced almost nothing on the spot, no breakthrough theorem, no working system born that summer. What it did was assemble the people who would lead the field for a generation, McCarthy and Minsky above all, who went on to found the AI laboratories at Stanford and MIT, alongside Allen Newell and Herbert Simon, who arrived already carrying the Logic Theorist, an early program that proved theorems in symbolic logic and is often called the first working AI program. The meeting set the agenda, the vocabulary, and the social network of a new discipline, turning scattered efforts into a coherent enterprise with a shared name and a shared set of questions.

The intellectual stance that crystallized at Dartmouth shaped AI for decades, and so did its blind spot. The founders bet that intelligence could be achieved by manipulating symbols according to explicit rules, the program now called symbolic AI, and that confident logic-and-search vision drove the expert-system era and its real successes. But the timelines went unmet, and the gap between the founders' optimism and what the technology could deliver helped bring on the first AI winter. The deeper irony is that they bet on the wrong half: they treated reasoning as the hard core of intelligence and perception as a detail, almost the exact inversion of which problems turned out to be difficult, the pattern later named Moravec's paradox. The workshop is best remembered not as a moment of solved problems but as the founding act of a discipline that has spent seventy years relearning the limits of its founding assumptions.
