A machine can prove theorems and lose at picking up a cup. That inversion is Moravec's paradox, articulated in the 1980s by the roboticist Hans Moravec along with Marvin Minsky and others: the things that feel effortless to a person, recognizing a face, walking across a cluttered room, grasping a glass, catching the meaning of a spoken sentence, turn out to be staggeringly hard to build, while the things that feel like real intellectual labor, chess, formal logic, long arithmetic, turn out to be comparatively easy to mechanize. The hierarchy of difficulty for machines is roughly the mirror image of the one we feel from the inside.

The explanation is evolutionary, and it is the heart of the idea. Perception and motor control are the products of hundreds of millions of years of natural selection, optimized so thoroughly and run so far beneath awareness that they feel like nothing at all, the way you are not conscious of the staggering computation behind catching a ball. Abstract reasoning, by contrast, is an evolutionarily recent and thin veneer, a few thousand years of cultural practice laid over that ancient machinery, which is why it feels effortful and is exactly the kind of deliberate, step-by-step process early symbolic AI could imitate. As Moravec put it, we are most aware of the parts of our minds that are youngest and least reliable, and least aware of the oldest machinery that does the genuinely hard computation.

The paradox matters because it explains the actual shape of AI's history. Symbolic AI scored its early wins precisely on the abstract, rule-governed tasks the paradox predicts are easy: theorem provers, chess programs, the expert system. Meanwhile the sensorimotor problems stayed stubbornly unsolved for decades, which is why robust machine vision, speech, and dexterous robotics arrived so much later than chess engines that could already beat grandmasters. The paradox reframes a generation of apparent failure: the field was not failing at intelligence in general, it was bouncing off the specific parts of it that are hardest to mechanize, the parts we are least able to introspect.

Moravec's paradox connects tightly to the bitter lesson. The sensorimotor skills the paradox flags as hard are exactly the ones that resisted being hand-coded as rules and that finally yielded to learned, data-driven, compute-heavy methods: deep learning is what cracked vision and speech, not better hand-written rules. The two ideas together form one coherent picture of the field. Reasoning was easy to program and hard for evolution to grow; perception was easy for evolution to grow and hard to program; and the methods that finally conquered perception did so by learning it from data rather than by anyone writing it down. What feels simplest to us is what took evolution the longest, and that turned out to be what took AI the longest too.
