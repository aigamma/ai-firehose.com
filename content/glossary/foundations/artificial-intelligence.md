Artificial intelligence has a habit of disappearing the moment it succeeds. Once a problem genuinely yields, playing chess, recognizing speech, filtering spam, it tends to shed the name and get reclassified as ordinary computation, a reflex John McCarthy, who coined the term, captured in the complaint that "as soon as it works, no one calls it AI anymore." The pattern is common enough to have a name of its own, the AI effect, and it explains a confusion that trails the field everywhere: AI labels a frontier, the cognitive tasks machines cannot yet do reliably, more than it labels any fixed technology. What counts as AI in 2026 is not what counted in 1996.

Underneath the moving label sits a stable ambition. Artificial intelligence is the study and construction of systems that produce behavior we would call intelligent in a person: perceiving a scene, understanding a sentence, planning toward a goal, proving a theorem. The discipline took its name at the 1956 dartmouth workshop, whose proposal bet that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." That conjecture, optimistic and still unproven in full, has organized seventy years of work.

AI is less a single method than a container for rival answers to one question: how do you get a machine to act intelligently? The field has cycled between two. symbolic AI writes knowledge down as explicit rules and logical symbols and reasons by manipulating them; connectionism builds many simple units that learn statistical patterns from data, the lineage that runs from the perceptron to the modern neural network. Most of this glossary is the descendants of those two families, and most of AI's history is the contest between them.

The other distinction that heads off confusion is narrow versus general. Every system deployed in the world today is narrow: it does one bounded thing, a spam filter, a chess engine, a language model, however superhuman it looks inside that band. Artificial general intelligence, a single system with human-like flexibility across arbitrary tasks, remains a goal rather than a built artifact. Mistaking impressive narrow performance for general competence is the most common error in public discussion of the field, and it is the AI effect running the other way: we over-credit the unsolved and quietly discount the solved.

The honest summary is that AI is an aspiration and a toolkit more than a finished science. It has produced enormous practical capability, vision, translation, code, conversation, without settling what intelligence actually is, which is why its defining benchmark for decades was a behavioral dodge, the turing-test, rather than a definition. The moving boundary is not a flaw in the vocabulary. It is the field reporting, every time it succeeds, that it has converted one more piece of mystery into mechanism, and then gone looking for the next piece.
