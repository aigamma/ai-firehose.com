Artificial intelligence is the study and engineering of systems that exhibit behavior we would call intelligent if a person produced it: recognizing a face, translating a sentence, planning a route, proving a theorem, or beating a champion at a game. The name dates to the 1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project, where John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester proposed that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." That conjecture, optimistic and still unproven in full, has organized the field ever since.

AI matters because it is the broad container for nearly every other concept in this glossary. Machine learning, neural networks, deep learning, and expert systems are not rivals to AI but successive answers to the same question: how do you get a machine to behave intelligently? The field has cycled between two big strategies. One, symbolic AI, encodes knowledge as explicit rules and logical symbols a program manipulates. The other, connectionism, builds many simple units that learn statistical patterns from data, the lineage that runs through the perceptron and the modern neural network.

A useful distinction separates narrow AI from general AI. Narrow AI solves one bounded task and is everything deployed in the world today: a spam filter, a chess engine, a speech recognizer, a language model. Artificial general intelligence, by contrast, would match human flexibility across arbitrary tasks and remains a research goal rather than a built artifact. Confusing the two is the most common error in public discussion of the field, because impressive narrow performance is routinely mistaken for general competence.

Measuring intelligence in a machine is itself hard, which is why the Turing test was proposed so early as an operational stand-in: if a machine's conversation is indistinguishable from a person's, treat it as thinking. Whether that is the right test, or whether passing it demonstrates understanding at all, is a live philosophical question rather than a settled one. The honest summary is that AI names an aspiration and a toolkit more than a finished science. It has delivered enormous practical capability without resolving what intelligence ultimately is.
