An AI winter is a stretch in which optimism about artificial intelligence collapses, money and attention drain away, and progress stalls. The term, coined by analogy with nuclear winter, describes a recurring boom-and-bust pattern: a striking early result fuels grand promises, the promises outrun what the technology can actually deliver, funders lose patience, and the field contracts until a new idea revives it. Understanding this cycle is part of being literate about AI, because it tempers the hype that accompanies every advance and explains why veterans of the field treat sweeping claims with caution.

The first major winter unfolded in the 1970s. Two blows stand out. In 1969 Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert's analysis of the perceptron emphasized what single-layer networks could not do, which helped freeze connectionist research for years. Then in 1973 the Lighthill report, commissioned by the British government, concluded that AI had failed to deliver on its promises, particularly its inability to handle the combinatorial explosion of real-world problems, and recommended deep cuts. Funding from agencies in both Britain and the United States fell sharply.

A second winter arrived in the late 1980s and early 1990s, and its cause is instructive. The commercial boom in the expert system had built an industry around specialized hardware and rule-based software that captured human know-how. These systems proved expensive to build and maintain, brittle at the edges of their hand-coded rules, and unable to learn, while cheaper general-purpose computers undercut the specialized machines. The market for them collapsed, taking much of the surrounding enthusiasm with it. This winter is the clearest illustration that a technology can fail commercially because of its intrinsic limits, not merely because of overpromising.

The lesson AI winters teach is durable: progress in the field has been uneven, driven by paradigm shifts rather than steady accumulation, and each thaw came from a genuinely new approach rather than more effort on the stalled one. The connectionist revival of the 1980s broke the first freeze, and the deep learning surge of the 2010s, powered by large datasets and fast hardware, ended the long malaise that followed the second. The pattern argues for judging AI by demonstrated capability rather than projection, and it is why the bitter lesson's emphasis on scalable, general methods over hand-built cleverness reads, in part, as a diagnosis of what caused the winters in the first place.
