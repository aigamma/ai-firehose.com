An AI winter is what happens after an AI summer. A striking result fuels sweeping promises, the promises outrun what the technology can actually do, funders and the public lose patience, money and talent drain away, and the field contracts until some new idea revives it. The term was coined by analogy with nuclear winter, and the cycle it names is the most important fact about AI's history, because it keeps recurring. Knowing the pattern is what lets a literate observer meet every breathless claim, including the ones being made right now, with the calibrated skepticism the field's own past recommends.

The first freeze set in during the 1970s, and two blows stand out. In 1969 Minsky and Papert's analysis of the perceptron emphasized what single-layer networks could not do, which helped stall connectionist research for years. Then in 1973 the British government's Lighthill report concluded that AI had broadly failed to deliver, singling out its helplessness against the combinatorial explosion of real-world problems, and recommended deep cuts; funding fell sharply on both sides of the Atlantic. The confident timelines of the Dartmouth-workshop generation, that the core problems would yield in a few years, had simply gone unmet.

The second winter, in the late 1980s and early 1990s, is the more instructive because it was a commercial collapse, not just an academic one. The boom in the expert system had built a whole industry around specialized hardware and rule-based software, and it imploded: the systems were expensive to build and maintain, brittle at the edges of their hand-coded rules, unable to learn, and undercut by cheaper general-purpose computers. This is the clearest case of a technology failing in the market because of its intrinsic limits rather than mere overpromising, and it took much of the field's credibility down with it.

The durable lesson is in how the thaws arrived. Each winter ended not through more effort on the stalled idea but through a genuinely different approach: the connectionist revival of the 1980s broke the first freeze, and the deep learning surge of the 2010s, powered by large datasets and fast hardware, ended the long malaise after the second. Progress in AI has been lurching and paradigm-driven rather than a steady accumulation, which is exactly why projecting today's trajectory in a straight line is the error each winter punished. The bitter lesson's preference for scalable, general methods over hand-built cleverness reads, in part, as a diagnosis of what caused the winters in the first place.
