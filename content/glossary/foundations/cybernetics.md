The central image of cybernetics is a system watching the consequences of its own actions and adjusting. A thermostat senses that the room is too cold and turns on the heat; a hand reaches for a cup and refines its motion as the eyes track the closing gap; an animal, an engine, and an economy all steer toward a goal by feeding their own error back into their next move. Norbert Wiener gave this circular, self-correcting logic a name in his 1948 book, whose subtitle, "control and communication in the animal and the machine," is the field's definition, and he argued that one shared mathematics of signals, control, and information governs steering engines and nervous systems and societies alike.

Cybernetics matters to the history of AI because it was the immediate intellectual precursor, the dominant framework for thinking about intelligent machines in the decade before AI had a name. It grew out of wartime work on automatic gun-laying, where a system had to track an aircraft and predict its motion, and it fused that engineering of control with new theories of information and computation and with neurophysiology. The 1943 model of McCulloch and Pitts, which treated idealized neurons as logical switches, was a cybernetic landmark, and the interdisciplinary Macy conferences of the 1940s and 1950s gathered the mathematicians, engineers, psychologists, and biologists who built the field.

Then the field split, and the split shaped AI for decades. When the Dartmouth-workshop coined "artificial intelligence" in 1956, it was in part marking out territory distinct from this older, more biological tradition. The new symbolic school located intelligence in the manipulation of explicit symbols and logical rules, a top-down program that bracketed the body and the control loop as irrelevant to thought. Cybernetics was the opposite, bottom-up and embodied, treating intelligence as adaptive regulation that emerges from an agent coupled to its environment through continuous feedback. As symbolic AI captured the funding and the prestige, cybernetics faded as a named discipline through the 1960s and 1970s, its insights dispersing into control theory, robotics, and the study of complex systems.

Cybernetics matters now because its core ideas came back, often without its name, at the heart of modern machine learning. The perceptron and the whole connectionist program descend directly from its neural modeling, so today's neural network is in a real sense a cybernetic artifact. Its feedback principle is the skeleton of reinforcement learning, where an agent acts, observes a reward, and adjusts, exactly Wiener's loop of action and correction; gradient descent is itself a feedback process that senses error and corrects the parameters against it. The embodied, environment-coupled view it championed anticipated today's work in robotics and embodied cognition. Cybernetics is the road AI did not take at its founding that turned out to be a road back, and seeing the lineage clarifies why so much of modern AI, from learning by error correction to agents shaped by their environments, looks less like the logic-and-search dream of the Dartmouth founders and more like the feedback machines Wiener described first.
