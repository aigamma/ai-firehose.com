The Turing test was introduced by Alan Turing in his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." Turing found the question "can machines think" too vague to answer and replaced it with a concrete game. A human interrogator exchanges typed messages with two hidden partners, one a person and one a machine, and tries to tell which is which. If the interrogator cannot do better than chance, Turing argued, we have no principled reason to deny that the machine is thinking. He called this setup the imitation game.

The test matters because it set the agenda for evaluating artificial intelligence and reframed the whole question behaviorally. Rather than demanding a definition of consciousness or understanding, Turing proposed that observable performance is what we can actually measure, so it is what the standard should rest on. This sidestep was liberating for early research: it gave the field a finish line that did not require first solving the philosophy of mind. It also embedded a bias toward language as the proving ground of intelligence, which is why conversation has remained a central benchmark from the first chatbots to today's large language models.

The test is deliberately about indistinguishability, not mechanism. It does not care whether the machine reasons with explicit symbols, as in symbolic AI, or with learned statistical associations, as in connectionism; it judges only the output. That neutrality is a strength for measurement and a weakness for interpretation, because a system can imitate conversation convincingly without any of the comprehension a human brings. Joseph Weizenbaum's 1966 ELIZA, a simple pattern-matching script, already fooled some users, which Weizenbaum himself took as a warning rather than a triumph.

This gap between imitating and understanding is the test's most debated feature. John Searle's Chinese Room argument contends that manipulating symbols by rule, however convincingly, is not the same as understanding their meaning, so passing the test would not prove a mind. Whether one accepts that critique or not, the Turing test endures less as a serious finish line for modern AI than as the founding statement of a stance: judge intelligence by what a system does, and remember that fluent behavior is evidence of capability, not proof of understanding.
