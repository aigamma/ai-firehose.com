Mode connectivity is a discovery about the shape of the loss landscape in deep networks. Intuition from low-dimensional optimization says that different good solutions sit in separate valleys divided by high ridges, so moving from one to another would require climbing over a barrier of high loss. In deep networks this is false. Garipov and colleagues, and independently Draxler and colleagues, showed in 2018 that two independently trained models can be joined by a simple curve through parameter space, often just a bent path of one or two segments, along which the loss stays as low as at the endpoints. The minima are not isolated; they are linked by low-loss tunnels.

The phenomenon comes in two strengths, and the difference matters. General mode connectivity lets the connecting path curve, and almost any pair of minima can be joined that way. Linear mode connectivity is the stronger, rarer claim that the straight line between two solutions stays low-loss, with no barrier at all. Two networks trained from scratch with different random seeds are usually not linearly connected, but they often become so if they share the early part of training before branching, an observation that ties directly to the lottery ticket hypothesis, since a winning ticket can be characterized as a subnetwork stable to the noise of training in exactly this linear sense.

Part of why barriers seem to vanish is symmetry, and it is the deepest part of the story. A network's hidden units can be permuted without changing the function it computes, so each solution is really one representative of a large family of equivalent points scattered across parameter space, and much of the apparent separation between two independently trained models is just a mismatch of these permutations. Align one network's units to the other's first, and the barrier often collapses so that even from-scratch runs look linearly connected, which suggests that, up to symmetry, the good solutions may occupy essentially one connected region.

Mode connectivity reshaped the mental picture of the loss landscape and carries practical consequences. Because low-loss paths exist, you can build ensembles cheaply by sampling diverse models along a connecting curve rather than training many from scratch, and weight averaging along such paths finds wide, flat minima that generalize well. Understanding which models can be merged underpins model-soup methods and informs when two checkpoints can be combined or distilled together through knowledge distillation. The topic stays active because it links the geometry of the landscape, the role of symmetry, and the empirical reliability of training into one structural fact: in high dimensions, the good solutions of a deep network are connected.
