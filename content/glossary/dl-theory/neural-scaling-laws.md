Neural scaling laws are the observation that the test loss of a large network shrinks as a smooth power law in three quantities: the number of parameters, the number of training tokens, and the total compute spent. Plotted on log-log axes, loss against any of these forms a nearly straight line over many orders of magnitude. Kaplan and colleagues at OpenAI documented this for language models in 2020, and the regularity was strong enough that one could fit the curve on small models and extrapolate the loss of a model hundreds of times larger before spending the compute to build it. The general entry on scaling laws covers the broad principle; this one focuses on the precise empirical relationships and how they are used.

The laws turned model building from guesswork into forecasting, and the most consequential lesson is about how to spend a fixed budget. Each power law has an exponent saying how fast loss falls as you add resources, and the exponents are small, so progress is steady but expensive: large multiplicative increases in compute buy modest additive drops in loss. The 2022 Chinchilla result from Hoffmann and colleagues at DeepMind refined the picture by jointly optimizing model size and data, and found the earlier large models had been badly undertrained: for compute-optimal training, parameters and training tokens should grow in roughly equal proportion. That correction reset the field's sense of how big a model should be for a given budget and how much data it needs.

The appeal is predictive control. Before committing to a costly run, a team fits the scaling curve on a ladder of small models, reads off the size and dataset a target loss requires, and the compute that will cost, which makes pretraining a planned investment rather than a gamble. It is part of why the field leaned so hard into scale: the laws promised that more compute, applied to more data and larger models, would keep paying off, a quantitative echo of the bitter lesson that general methods riding on computation tend to win.

The laws describe a smooth average, and the texture around that average is itself a research subject. Aggregate loss falls predictably, yet performance on specific tasks can stay flat and then jump sharply with scale, the pattern discussed under emergent ability, consistent with a smoothly improving loss but not obvious from it. Scaling laws are also not eternal: they assume fresh high-quality data and bend when data runs short or repeats, when the architecture changes, or when the loss nears its irreducible floor. Extending them to data-constrained regimes, to fine-tuning and transfer, and to modalities beyond text is ongoing work, but as a tool for forecasting and allocating resources they remain one of the most practically important findings in modern deep learning.
