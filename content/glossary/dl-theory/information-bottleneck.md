The information bottleneck is an information-theoretic answer to a deceptively basic question: what makes a representation good? Formulated by Tishby, Pereira, and Bialek in 1999, it asks a learner to find an internal representation of the input that is maximally informative about the output label while being maximally compressed about the input itself. These two goals pull in opposite directions, so the principle is stated as a tradeoff: minimize the mutual information between input and representation, subject to keeping the mutual information between representation and target high, with a single parameter setting the exchange rate between compression and prediction.

The appeal is that this defines "relevant" information with no reference to any particular architecture. The ideal representation is a minimal sufficient statistic of the input for the task: it discards every detail of the input that does not help predict the label and keeps every detail that does. Nuisance variation, the parts of the input uncorrelated with the answer, gets squeezed out at the bottleneck, which is exactly the kind of invariance we want a model to learn, and it connects the principle to entropy, KL divergence, and the broader project of describing learning in the currency of bits.

The principle drew wide attention when Tishby and Shwartz-Ziv proposed in 2017 that deep networks trained by gradient descent pass through two phases visible in the information plane: a short fitting phase where the layers gain information about the label, followed by a long compression phase where they discard information about the input. In this telling generalization comes from compression, and a deep network implements a chain of successive bottlenecks. The claim was influential and also sharply contested, since the measured dynamics depend on how mutual information is estimated and on the choice of activation function, and the debate usefully sharpened the field's care about information-theoretic measurement in high dimensions.

Beyond the explanatory story, the information bottleneck is a usable training objective. Deep variational versions, closely related to the variational autoencoder, optimize a tractable bound on the bottleneck tradeoff and have been used to build representations that are robust, compact, and less prone to memorizing spurious detail. The framework also names its own failure modes: compress too hard and the representation keeps too little to predict well, a form of representation collapse, while too little compression leaves nuisance information that hurts generalization. It offers one unified axis, bits kept versus bits discarded, along which to reason about both representation quality and the bias toward simple solutions.
