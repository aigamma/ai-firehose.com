An emergent ability is a skill a large model has and a smaller model of the same family does not, where the transition with scale looks like a sudden onset rather than a gradual climb. Below some size the model performs at or near chance; above it, performance rises sharply. The term was popularized by Wei and colleagues in 2022, who catalogued tasks, multi-step arithmetic, certain reasoning and word problems, the ability to follow in-context instructions, where large language model performance stayed flat across many sizes and then jumped. The defining feature is unpredictability: you could not have forecast the ability by extrapolating the trend from smaller models.

Emergent ability is the counterpart to the smoothness of neural scaling laws, and the tension between them is the heart of the topic. Aggregate loss falls predictably as a power law, yet individual task accuracy can be flat then steep, and there is no contradiction. A task that requires several sub-steps all to be correct can have a success rate that stays near zero while the underlying per-step competence improves smoothly, then climbs quickly once each step becomes reliable enough that their product crosses into success. Emergence in this sense is what a smoothly improving model looks like through a sharp, all-or-nothing metric, which links it to the broader notion of emergence and, within a single model over training, to grokking.

That observation became a sharp critique. Schaeffer and colleagues argued in 2023 that many reported emergent abilities are partly artifacts of the chosen metric: discontinuous or thresholded scores, like exact-match accuracy on a long sequence, manufacture sharp jumps, and replacing them with smooth, partial-credit metrics often turns the same data into a gradual, predictable curve. This does not erase the practical reality that capabilities appear with scale, but it reframes the sharpness as frequently a property of measurement rather than of the model, and it raised the field's standard for claiming a true discontinuity.

Whether sharp or smooth, emergent ability matters because it bears directly on forecasting and safety. If useful and potentially hazardous capabilities can switch on at scales not yet trained, evaluating only today's models gives limited assurance about tomorrow's, which motivates careful capability evaluations and red-teaming as models grow. It also interacts with how a capability is elicited, since techniques like chain-of-thought prompting can surface reasoning skills in large models that simpler prompting leaves hidden, so what counts as emergent depends partly on how the model is queried. The practical upshot is to treat scale as a source of qualitatively new behavior to test for, while measuring it with metrics that do not invent discontinuities on their own.
