Reinforcement learning rests on an audacious claim about what a goal even is. Rich Sutton stated it as the reward hypothesis: that "all of what we mean by goals and purposes can be well thought of as the maximization of the expected value of the cumulative sum of a received scalar signal." If that holds, you never have to tell an agent how to behave; you only define a reward, and the right behavior falls out of the agent's struggle to accumulate as much of it as possible. The agent observes the state of an environment, takes an action, receives a reward and a new state, and over many such steps learns a policy, a mapping from states to actions, that maximizes reward over time. There is no labeled dataset of correct moves; the only teacher is the reward, which may be sparse and may arrive long after the action that earned it.

This framing reaches problems the other paradigms cannot state cleanly: control, planning, and games, where success is a sequence of choices rather than a single prediction. It produced systems that beat human champions at Go and chess, that steer stratospheric balloons and cool datacenters, and, through reinforcement learning from human feedback, that turned raw language models into assistants by optimizing a reward learned from human preference. Wherever the goal is a behavior tuned over time rather than a one-shot answer, this is the natural shape.

Two difficulties have no analogue in the static datasets of the other paradigms. The first is credit assignment: when a reward finally lands, which of the dozens of earlier actions deserve the credit or the blame? The standard answer is to learn a value function that estimates expected future reward from each state, then steer the policy toward higher-valued actions, propagating credit backward through time. The second is the exploration-exploitation tradeoff: an agent must keep trying unfamiliar actions to learn whether they pay better, even while exploiting the ones already known to work, because a learner that only exploits will lock onto the first decent strategy it finds and never discover the great one.

Reinforcement learning borrows freely from the rest of the field: deep reinforcement learning uses a neural network, trained much as in supervised learning, to approximate the policy or value function when the states are too many to enumerate. But its founding strength is also its sharpest hazard. If every goal is a reward to maximize, the agent will maximize exactly the reward you wrote, not the one you meant, and the gap between them is reward hacking, the boat-racing agent that spins in circles collecting points forever instead of finishing the race. The reward hypothesis is what makes reinforcement learning so general, and it is also why specifying reward correctly turns out to be one of the central unsolved problems in building systems that do what we actually intend.
