Variational inference approximates a probability distribution too hard to compute exactly, most often the posterior over a model's hidden variables. Exact Bayesian inference requires an integral over all possible values of those hidden variables, the normalizing constant called the evidence, and for almost any interesting model that integral has no closed form. Variational inference sidesteps it by recasting inference as optimization: propose a family of simpler, tractable distributions and search within it for the member closest to the true posterior. Inference becomes a fitting problem.

This is what makes Bayesian reasoning scale. The historical alternative, Markov chain Monte Carlo, draws samples that converge to the exact posterior but can be slow and hard to diagnose on large datasets; variational inference trades that asymptotic exactness for speed, replacing sampling with gradient-based optimization that rides on the same hardware and autodifferentiation tools as the rest of modern machine learning. This is what lets probabilistic models be trained on millions of examples, and it is the engine that made deep generative models with latent variables practical.

The machinery has two pieces: a measure of closeness and a quantity to optimize. Closeness is the KL divergence from the approximating distribution to the true posterior, an asymmetric gap that is zero only when the two match, but because the true posterior is unknown that divergence cannot be minimized directly. The algebra resolves it neatly: minimizing the KL divergence is exactly equivalent to maximizing a tractable objective, the evidence lower bound, or ELBO, which depends only on quantities the model can evaluate. Practitioners therefore maximize the ELBO, often with stochastic gradients, and the approximation tightens as the bound rises. A common simplification, the mean-field family, treats the hidden variables as independent, which makes the optimization easy at the cost of ignoring correlations among them.

Variational inference is the optimization-flavored counterpart to the sampling-flavored Monte Carlo methods, and it generalizes older algorithms: expectation maximization is the special case where the approximate posterior is allowed to be exact, so the two share the same lower-bound scaffolding. Its most consequential modern application is the variational autoencoder, which pairs variational inference with a neural network, an encoder outputting the parameters of the approximate posterior over a latent space, the whole system trained end to end by maximizing the ELBO. That marriage of variational inference and deep learning is what turned approximate Bayesian inference from a niche statistical technique into a mainstream tool for generative modeling.
