Metropolis-Hastings is the general-purpose recipe for sampling from a distribution you can evaluate only up to an unknown normalizing constant, the situation in nearly every interesting Bayesian model. It builds a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target by alternating two steps: it proposes a candidate next state by drawing from a proposal distribution centered on the current state, then accepts or rejects that candidate according to an acceptance probability designed so the chain spends time in each region in proportion to the target density. If the candidate is rejected, the chain stays where it is and the current state is repeated.

The acceptance rule is the heart of the method and the reason it works without ever needing the normalizing constant. The probability of accepting a move is the minimum of one and a ratio: the target density at the candidate over the target density at the current point, corrected by the ratio of proposal densities in the two directions. Because the unknown constant appears in both numerator and denominator of the target-density ratio, it cancels exactly, so only relative, unnormalized densities are ever required. This single fact is what makes Bayesian inference computable: the intractable evidence integral in the denominator of the posterior never has to be evaluated, because Metropolis-Hastings needs only ratios of the unnormalized posterior, the likelihood times the prior.

Metropolis-Hastings is the umbrella under which most Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithms sit. Gibbs sampling is the special case where the proposal is each variable's exact conditional and every move is accepted; more sophisticated members design smarter proposals to explore faster, with Hamiltonian Monte Carlo simulating physical dynamics using the gradient of the log target to make long, informed, high-acceptance moves and the No-U-Turn Sampler automating its tuning. These gradient-based variants are what power modern probabilistic programming, letting practitioners specify a model and obtain posterior samples without hand-deriving an algorithm.

The practical art of Metropolis-Hastings lies in choosing the proposal. Steps too small make the chain crawl, exploring slowly and producing highly correlated samples; steps too large get rejected constantly, so the chain stalls in place. The acceptance rate is the key diagnostic, and theory suggests tuning it to a moderate value, often near a quarter for random-walk proposals, to balance these failure modes. Like all such methods it gives correlated rather than independent samples, requires a burn-in period to forget its starting point, and demands convergence diagnostics before its output can be trusted. When that overhead is too costly, variational inference offers a faster but only approximate alternative, trading the asymptotic exactness of Metropolis-Hastings for the speed of optimization.
