Metropolis-Hastings is the general-purpose recipe for sampling from a probability distribution that you can evaluate only up to an unknown normalizing constant, which is the situation in nearly every interesting Bayesian model. It builds a [Markov chain](markov-chain) whose stationary distribution is the target by alternating two steps. First it proposes a candidate next state by drawing from a proposal distribution centered on the current state. Then it accepts or rejects that candidate according to an acceptance probability designed so that the chain spends time in each region in proportion to the target density; if the candidate is rejected, the chain simply stays where it is and the current state is repeated.

The acceptance rule is the heart of the method and the reason it works without ever needing the normalizing constant. The probability of accepting a move is the minimum of one and a ratio: the target density at the candidate divided by the target density at the current point, corrected by the ratio of proposal densities in the two directions. Because the unknown constant appears in both the numerator and the denominator of the target-density ratio, it cancels exactly, so only relative, unnormalized densities are ever required. This single fact is what makes [Bayesian inference](bayesian-inference) computable: the intractable evidence integral in the denominator of the posterior never has to be evaluated, because Metropolis-Hastings needs only ratios of the unnormalized posterior, the likelihood times the prior.

Metropolis-Hastings is the umbrella under which most [Markov chain Monte Carlo](markov-chain-monte-carlo) algorithms sit. [Gibbs sampling](gibbs-sampling) is the special case where the proposal is each variable's exact conditional and every move is accepted. More sophisticated members of the family design smarter proposals to explore faster: Hamiltonian Monte Carlo simulates physical dynamics using the gradient of the log target to make long, informed moves with high acceptance, and the No-U-Turn Sampler automates its tuning. These gradient-based variants are what power modern probabilistic programming systems, letting practitioners specify a model and obtain posterior samples without hand-deriving an algorithm.

The practical art of Metropolis-Hastings lies in choosing the proposal. Steps that are too small make the chain crawl, exploring the distribution slowly and producing highly correlated samples; steps that are too large get rejected constantly, so the chain stalls in place. The acceptance rate is the key diagnostic, and theory suggests tuning it to a moderate value, often near a quarter for random-walk proposals, to balance these failure modes. Like all such methods it provides correlated samples rather than independent ones, requires a burn-in period to forget its starting point, and demands convergence diagnostics before its output can be trusted. When that overhead is too costly, [variational inference](variational-inference) offers a faster but only approximate alternative, trading the asymptotic exactness of Metropolis-Hastings for the speed of optimization.
