Markov chain Monte Carlo, or MCMC, draws samples from a probability distribution you can evaluate up to a constant but cannot sample from directly. The central trick is to construct a Markov chain, a random walk whose next state depends only on the current one, carefully designed so its long-run equilibrium distribution is exactly the target. Run the chain long enough and the states it visits become samples from the target, even when the target is a high-dimensional posterior with no closed form. You do not find the distribution; you build a process that settles into it.

MCMC is the workhorse of exact-in-the-limit Bayesian inference, and the reason is one property. The hard part of any Bayesian calculation is the normalizing constant, the integral over all parameter values, and MCMC never needs it, because the algorithms depend only on ratios of the unnormalized density, in which the constant cancels. That single fact unlocks posteriors over models that would otherwise be impossible to handle, and it lets you estimate any expectation under the posterior, a predictive mean, a tail probability, a credible interval, simply by averaging a function over the collected samples. For decades, before scalable alternatives existed, MCMC was effectively how Bayesian statistics was done.

The mechanics are easiest to see in the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. From the current state it proposes a candidate move, then accepts or rejects it with a probability built from the ratio of target densities at the two points, so moves toward higher-density regions are usually accepted and moves toward lower-density regions sometimes are, which is exactly what forces the chain's equilibrium to be the target. Gibbs sampling is the special case that updates one variable at a time from its conditional, and modern gradient-aware variants like Hamiltonian Monte Carlo and the No-U-Turn Sampler use the gradient of the log-density to propose long, efficient moves, which is why they power general-purpose probabilistic programming. In every case the early samples, collected before the chain reaches equilibrium, are discarded as burn-in, and the practitioner must check that the chain has mixed and explored the whole distribution.

MCMC sits opposite variational inference on the central tradeoff of approximate Bayesian computation. MCMC is asymptotically exact but can be slow, sequential, and hard to diagnose, especially on large datasets and highly correlated posteriors; variational inference is fast and scalable but only approximate. It is closely related to importance sampling, another Monte Carlo estimator, but MCMC's adaptive, density-following walk explores high-dimensional spaces far more efficiently than drawing independent weighted samples from a fixed proposal. Knowing when a problem calls for the exactness of MCMC versus the speed of variational methods is one of the practical judgments at the center of probabilistic machine learning.
