A tangent space is the flat approximation to a curved manifold at one specific point. Picture a sphere with a plane balanced against it, touching at a single point: that plane is the tangent space there, and it collects every possible direction and speed you could set off in from that point. For a one-dimensional curve the tangent space is the tangent line; for a surface it is the tangent plane; in general it is a flat vector space of the same dimension as the manifold, attached at that point and valid for an infinitesimal neighborhood around it. Although the manifold is curved, the tangent space is perfectly flat, so adding vectors, scaling them, and taking inner products all work there even when they make no sense on the curved surface itself.

The tangent space exists to solve a basic problem: you cannot do ordinary vector arithmetic on a curved surface, because adding two points on a sphere does not give a point on the sphere, and there is no global coordinate grid in which differentiation behaves simply. But you can do linear algebra in the flat tangent space and then map the result back to the manifold. This is the same move that underlies all of differential calculus, replacing a curved object with its best linear approximation, applied pointwise across an entire space: the velocity of a path is a tangent vector, the gradient of a function on the manifold is a tangent vector, and the derivative of a map between manifolds is a linear map between their tangent spaces.

On a Riemannian manifold the tangent space is where the geometry actually lives, because the metric tensor is defined as an inner product on each tangent space. That inner product is what measures the length of a tangent vector and the angle between two of them, and integrating those lengths along a path gives the distances that define geodesics. This is exactly why tangent spaces are indispensable in geometry-aware learning: Riemannian optimization computes a gradient in the tangent space, steps along it, and maps the step back onto the manifold through the exponential map, which follows the geodesic in that direction, the recipe behind training hyperbolic embeddings and optimizing over rotation or covariance matrices without ever leaving the valid set.

The tangent space is also where the manifold's curvature reveals itself, through how the spaces fail to line up. Because each point has its own tangent space, comparing a vector at one point with a vector at another is not automatic; you must carry the vector along a path by parallel transport, and on a curved manifold the result depends on the path taken. That path-dependence is precisely curvature. So the tangent space is both the workshop where flat calculations are done and, through the way neighboring tangent spaces twist relative to each other, the place where the bending of the underlying space becomes measurable.
