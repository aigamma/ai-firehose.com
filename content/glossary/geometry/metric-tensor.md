The metric tensor is what tells you how to measure on a curved space, point by point. At each location it takes two tangent vectors and returns a number that plays the role of their dot product, represented as a symmetric positive-definite matrix, and from it the length of a single vector and the angle between two of them follow immediately. The decisive feature is that this rule can vary from place to place: the ruler and protractor you use here may differ from the ones a short distance away. A bare manifold knows only its smooth shape; supplying a metric tensor is exactly what upgrades it to a Riemannian manifold and gives it genuine geometry, which is why the metric is often called simply "the metric."

Everything quantitative about a curved space is computed from its metric, the one object from which all the rest is derived. The length of a path is the integral of the metric-induced speed of its tangent vector; the shortest path between two points is a geodesic, and its length is the geodesic distance; and the way the metric forces nearby geodesics to converge or diverge is precisely what curvature measures. Distance, straightness, and bending are not independent facts about a space, they are all consequences of the metric, which is why specifying the metric is the same as specifying the geometry. In flat Euclidean space the metric is just the identity matrix everywhere, recovering ordinary Pythagorean distance as the trivial special case.

This means the metric is also a modeling choice with real consequences, because choosing it is choosing what near and far mean. The default Euclidean or cosine comparison of embedding vectors is the choice of a trivial constant metric, but it is a choice: a learned or position-dependent metric, sometimes a pullback metric induced by how a decoder maps a latent space into data space, reveals that the honest distance between two representations is generally not the naive straight-line one, and that the latent space is best read as a curved manifold whose metric the simple distance only approximates. Distance metric learning is, in this language, the explicit fitting of a metric tensor so semantically similar items come out close.

The most striking example of the metric setting the meaning of distance is the Fisher information metric of information geometry, where the metric on a space of probability distributions makes distance correspond to statistical distinguishability rather than raw parameter difference, which is what makes the natural gradient natural. Hyperbolic embeddings are another instance, deliberately adopting a metric of negative curvature to give hierarchies the room they need. In every case the recurring lesson is that geometry is a modeling choice: pick the metric tensor, and you have picked what length, angle, and distance mean for your problem.
