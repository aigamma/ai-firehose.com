Parallel transport solves a problem that does not arise in flat space: how to move a vector from one point of a curved manifold to another without rotating it more than the geometry forces. In Euclidean space the question is trivial, because you can slide a vector anywhere while keeping its components fixed, so vectors at different points live in the same space and compare directly. On a curved manifold this breaks down. Each point has its own tangent space, the flat plane of velocities there, and there is no automatic way to identify a vector in one with a vector in another. Parallel transport supplies that identification along a chosen path, carrying a tangent vector while holding it as constant as possible, turning it only as much as staying tangent to the curving surface demands and not one bit more.

What "as constant as possible" means is fixed by the connection, the structure on a Riemannian manifold that says how to differentiate a vector field as you move. The connection defines a covariant derivative, and a vector is parallel-transported along a curve when its covariant derivative along that curve is zero: it has no rate of change in the directions the manifold actually permits, and any apparent turning is the surface bending underneath it, not the vector swinging. Picture walking a path while holding an arrow and refusing to twist your wrist, letting only the tilt of the ground reorient it. The metric tensor singles out one natural connection, the Levi-Civita connection, which transports vectors so their lengths and mutual angles are preserved.

The deep fact is that parallel transport is path-dependent, and that path-dependence is precisely curvature. Carry a vector around a small closed loop and bring it back to where it started: on a flat surface it returns unchanged, but on a curved one it comes back rotated, by an angle proportional to the curvature the loop encloses. Transport the same vector to the same destination along two different routes and you generally get two different results. This mismatch after a round trip, called holonomy, is the coordinate-free signature of curvature, the same phenomenon that bends a geodesic and swells the angles of a triangle on a sphere beyond a straight angle. A geodesic can itself be defined this way: it is the curve that parallel-transports its own velocity, the manifold's notion of going straight ahead without turning.

For machine learning, parallel transport is what makes optimization and comparison on curved representation spaces coherent. In Riemannian optimization, where parameters live on a manifold such as the sphere, the rotation group, or the cone of positive-definite covariance matrices, momentum and other multi-step methods must combine gradient information gathered at different points. Those gradients are vectors in different tangent spaces and cannot simply be added; parallel transport moves the accumulated velocity to the current point first, which is what makes momentum on a manifold well defined at all. The same need arises in hyperbolic embeddings and other curved latent spaces, where transporting a feature or a gradient correctly is what keeps the geometry honest. Parallel transport is the practical bridge between the abstract picture of a connection in differential geometry and the concrete arithmetic of doing calculus on a curved model.
