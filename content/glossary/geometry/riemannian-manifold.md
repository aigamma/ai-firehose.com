A Riemannian manifold is a smooth shape that has been told how to measure itself. A bare manifold knows its topology but not its geometry: it can tell that it is a sphere rather than a plane, yet it cannot say how far apart two points are or whether two directions are perpendicular. The structure that supplies those answers is a Riemannian metric, a smoothly varying inner product on the tangent space at each point. Add that inner product and you can compute the length of any tangent vector and the angle between any two; withhold it and the space remains a shape with no ruler. The metric is the dividing line between knowing what a space looks like and knowing how to measure within it.

The metric is encoded by the metric tensor, a symmetric positive-definite matrix that can change from point to point across the manifold, and everything quantitative flows from it. The length of a curve is the integral of its tangent vector's metric-induced speed; the shortest curve between two points is a geodesic, and its length is the geodesic distance; and the way the metric forces nearby geodesics to spread or converge is exactly what curvature records. Because the metric varies, distances and straightest paths are not the naive Euclidean ones. The name honors Bernhard Riemann, who generalized Gauss's study of surfaces to spaces of any dimension and laid the groundwork Einstein later used to make gravity itself the curvature of a Riemannian spacetime.

Riemannian manifolds matter in machine learning wherever a representation space is not flat. The default assumption that embeddings live in flat Euclidean space is itself a trivial Riemannian metric, the identity everywhere, but richer choices unlock what flat space cannot reach. Hyperbolic embeddings place data on a negatively curved manifold so hierarchies fit with low distortion. Optimizing over constrained objects, rotation matrices, covariance matrices, directions on a unit sphere, is naturally posed as Riemannian optimization, where each gradient step is taken along a geodesic on the appropriate manifold so the iterate never leaves the valid set.

The most consequential example for statistics is information geometry, which treats a family of probability distributions as a Riemannian manifold whose metric is the Fisher information, so distance corresponds to statistical distinguishability rather than raw parameter difference. Natural gradient descent exploits exactly this geometry, correcting the ordinary gradient by the Fisher metric so updates respect how much the model's output actually moves. In every one of these settings the lesson repeats: choosing the metric tensor, and so the Riemannian manifold, is choosing what distance and direction mean for the problem at hand, which makes geometry not a fixed backdrop but a modeling decision.
