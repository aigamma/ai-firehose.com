Gaussian curvature is the single number that captures how a surface bends at a point, and it is built from a simple recipe. At any point a surface curves by different amounts in different directions; take the largest and smallest of those bendings, the principal curvatures, and multiply them. Where both bow the same way, as on a sphere or the end of an egg, the product is positive and the surface is dome-like. Where they bow opposite ways, as on a saddle or a mountain pass, one is positive and one negative, the product is negative, and the surface flares outward in two directions at once. Where either principal curvature is zero, as on a cylinder or a flat sheet, the product is zero and the surface is, in the sense that matters, flat.

The reason this particular number is celebrated rather than merely convenient is Gauss's Theorema Egregium of 1827, the "remarkable theorem," and it genuinely is remarkable. Gauss defined the quantity using how the surface sits in three-dimensional space, then proved the answer depends only on the surface's intrinsic geometry, the distances measured within the surface itself as encoded by the metric tensor. An inhabitant confined to the surface, able to measure lengths and angles along it but never to look from outside, could compute the Gaussian curvature everywhere. This is what makes it intrinsic, and it has a consequence everyone has held in their hands: a flat map of the Earth must distort distances, because the sphere has positive curvature and the plane has zero, and no bending without stretching can turn one into the other. A cylinder, by contrast, has zero Gaussian curvature, which is why paper rolls into a tube without wrinkling yet can never wrap a ball.

Gaussian curvature governs geodesics and triangles, binding it to the rest of differential geometry. On a positively curved surface, initially parallel geodesics converge and the angles of a geodesic triangle sum to more than a straight angle; on a negatively curved one they diverge and the angles sum to less. The Gauss-Bonnet theorem makes this global and exact: integrate the Gaussian curvature over a whole closed surface and you get a topological constant, two pi times the surface's Euler characteristic, untouched by how the surface is bent. Local shape and global topology are welded together by curvature. For surfaces, Gaussian curvature also matches, up to a factor, the scalar curvature that generalizes "how curved" to a Riemannian manifold of any dimension, so it is the two-dimensional anchor of the higher-dimensional machinery.

In machine learning the sign of curvature is a design decision about representation. Negatively curved hyperbolic space, where Gaussian curvature is everywhere negative, has volume that grows exponentially with radius, exactly the room tree-like and hierarchical data need, which is the geometric basis of hyperbolic embeddings. Positively curved spherical space is the natural home for directional data and for normalized embeddings compared by cosine similarity. Flat Euclidean space, with zero curvature, is the default most models assume by omission. The enduring lesson of Gaussian curvature, that some bending is intrinsic to a space and cannot be flattened away, is the same lesson that tells a practitioner when flat embeddings will distort their data and a curved geometry is required instead.
