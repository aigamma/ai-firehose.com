Fisher information quantifies how much a random observation tells you about an unknown parameter of the distribution it came from, and its definition is geometric: it is the expected curvature, the negative expected second derivative, of the log-likelihood around its peak. The picture is what makes it intuitive. If the log-likelihood has a sharp, narrow peak at the true parameter, then the data discriminate strongly between nearby parameter values and the Fisher information is large; if the peak is broad and flat, many parameter values explain the data almost equally well, the data are nearly uninformative, and the Fisher information is small. Curvature is information.

This connects directly to how precisely a parameter can be estimated, and it sets a hard limit no method can beat. The Cramer-Rao bound states that the variance of any unbiased estimator is at least the reciprocal of the Fisher information: more information means a lower floor on the achievable error. Maximum-likelihood estimators are attractive in part because they asymptotically attain this bound, which is the precise sense in which maximum likelihood is statistically efficient. The Fisher information thus translates directly into the width of confidence intervals, telling you not just the best estimate but how much to trust it.

For models with many parameters the Fisher information becomes a matrix, and reading it as an inner product on the tangent space at each distribution is the bridge to information geometry. It serves as the metric tensor on the manifold of probability distributions, defining distance there in terms of statistical distinguishability rather than raw parameter difference, and it is the unique such metric, up to scale, that does not depend on how the family is parameterized. This is the deep reason the same object governs both estimation accuracy and the geometry of distribution space: both are asking how sharply the data distinguish one distribution from its neighbors.

In modern machine learning the Fisher information is the object behind the natural gradient, which rescales updates by the inverse Fisher matrix so that each step moves the model's output distribution by a controlled, parameterization-invariant amount rather than chasing the arbitrary steepest direction in raw parameter space. Because the full matrix is enormous for a large network, practitioners use diagonal, block-diagonal, or Kronecker-factored approximations. The same matrix underlies Laplace approximations of the posterior and the methods that fight catastrophic forgetting in continual learning, where elastic weight consolidation uses the Fisher information to identify which parameters were critical to a previous task and anchors them while the network learns a new one. Across all of these, the Fisher information is the single quantity that says how much the data, or a task, truly constrains each parameter.
