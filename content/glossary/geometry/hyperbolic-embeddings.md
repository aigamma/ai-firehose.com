Hyperbolic embeddings start from a failure of the default. Almost all machine learning represents data in flat Euclidean space, and for hierarchical data that space runs out of room. A tree where every node has b children has on the order of b raised to the depth nodes at each level, an exponential explosion, and flat space, whose volume grows only polynomially with radius, simply does not have the room to hold it: pack a deep tree into a Euclidean plane and distant leaves get crushed together, their tree distances badly corrupted. The fix is to change the geometry of the container rather than add more dimensions, embedding the data in negatively curved hyperbolic space instead.

Hyperbolic space matters here because of one property that fits hierarchy as if made for it: its volume grows exponentially with radius. Move a fixed step outward from a point and the room available expands exponentially, exactly mirroring how a tree multiplies its nodes at each level. This means a hierarchy that would demand hundreds of Euclidean dimensions to embed with low distortion can fit into just two hyperbolic ones. Hyperbolic space is, in a precise sense, a continuous version of a tree: the geometry has the same exponential capacity the combinatorics need.

The most common working model is the Poincare ball, in which the entire infinite hyperbolic space is mapped into the interior of a unit ball, with the metric tensor stretching dramatically toward the rim so that the boundary sits at infinite distance from the center. Place the root of a hierarchy near the origin and push successive levels outward, and because space expands exponentially near the rim, each level finds exponentially more room than the last; the geodesic between two leaves naturally bows inward through their common ancestor, recovering tree distance as geometry. General concepts sit near the center, fine-grained instances near the edge, so the radial coordinate alone reports depth in the hierarchy without any extra labeling.

Hyperbolic embeddings suit taxonomies, ontologies, the hypernym graph of a language, social and citation networks, and any data with latent tree structure, often matching at two to ten dimensions what flat space cannot achieve at hundreds. The cost is that the machinery must be rebuilt for curved space: distance is the hyperbolic geodesic distance, gradients must be carried into the manifold's tangent space and the parameters updated by Riemannian optimization to keep points inside the ball, and even vector addition is redefined through the geometry, with some numerical care needed near the boundary where the metric blows up. They are the clearest applied lesson of the wider principle that the curvature of a representation space, not just its dimension, should match the structure of the data it holds: flat space for flat data, hyperbolic space for hierarchies.
