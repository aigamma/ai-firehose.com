Bayesian epistemology is the program that models rational belief using the mathematics of probability. Its first commitment is that belief is not all-or-nothing but graded: a rational agent holds each proposition with a degree of confidence, a credence, represented as a number between zero and one. Its second commitment is that these credences must obey the axioms of probability, on pain of irrationality. The standard justification is the Dutch book argument, which shows that an agent whose degrees of belief violate the probability axioms can be offered a set of bets each of which looks fair by the agent's own lights but which together guarantee a loss; conformity to the axioms is what makes one immune to such a sure-loss book. On this picture, probability theory is not merely a tool for describing chance in the world but the logic of partial belief, the extension of deductive consistency to uncertain reasoning.

The dynamic heart of the theory is a rule for changing one's mind. When an agent learns a piece of evidence, the rational response is to update every credence to its conditional probability given that evidence, a move called conditionalization, which is governed by bayes-theorem. The new degree of belief in a hypothesis is its prior degree of belief multiplied by how well the hypothesis predicted the evidence (the likelihood), renormalized. This single rule yields a complete account of learning: priors encode what you believed beforehand, likelihoods encode what each hypothesis says about the data, and the posterior is the disciplined synthesis. Bayesian confirmation theory builds the philosophy of science on top of this, explaining evidential support as the raising of a hypothesis's probability, and clarifying long-standing puzzles about when and how much an observation confirms a theory.

The framework's reach into machine learning is direct and deep, because much of statistical learning is Bayesian epistemology made computational. A model class is a space of hypotheses; a prior over parameters expresses initial preference; the likelihood is the probability the model assigns to the training data; and training that returns a posterior, or a maximum of it, is conditionalization carried out at scale. Regularization is the influence of a prior, model selection is the comparison of posterior probabilities, and the trade-off between fitting data and staying simple falls out of the arithmetic rather than being imposed by hand. The same logic recurs in cognitive science: the bayesian-brain hypothesis and predictive-coding propose that the mind itself is an approximate Bayesian inference engine, continually updating a probabilistic model of its world from sensory evidence, which would make Bayesian epistemology not only a norm for how agents ought to reason but a description of how natural intelligence does.

Bayesian epistemology faces serious and clarifying objections. The problem of priors asks where the initial credences come from: the theory constrains how to update but says little about what to believe before any evidence, and different priors can lead rational agents to different conclusions from the same data, which connects to the formal lesson of the no-free-lunch-theorem that no inductive bias is best for every problem. The problem of old evidence asks how already-known facts can confirm a new theory when their probability is already one. The assumption of logical omniscience, that a rational agent assigns probability one to all logical truths and instantly sees all consequences of its beliefs, is unattainable for any real or artificial reasoner with bounded computation. And the interpretation of the probabilities themselves is contested, with subjectivists treating them as personal degrees of belief disciplined only by coherence and objectivists seeking constraints that pin down uniquely rational credences.

For artificial intelligence the upshot is twofold. As a normative standard, Bayesian epistemology specifies what ideal inference under uncertainty would be, the target that practical learning algorithms approximate and against which their calibration and their handling of evidence can be judged; a system that reports confidence should, ideally, be Bayesian about it. As a descriptive and engineering paradigm, it supplies the language in which uncertainty is represented, propagated, and acted upon, from probabilistic models to the quantification of what a system does and does not know. The bounded, resource-limited character of real agents also makes Bayesian epistemology a natural meeting point with the computational-theory-of-mind, since the question of how an embodied, finite system can approximate ideal probabilistic reasoning is exactly the question of how to build, or to be, a rational mind under real constraints.
