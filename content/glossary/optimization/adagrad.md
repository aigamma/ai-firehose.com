AdaGrad, the adaptive gradient algorithm, was the first widely used optimizer to give every parameter its own learning rate rather than sharing one rate across the whole model. The idea is to accumulate, for each parameter separately, the sum of the squares of all the gradients it has ever received, and to scale that parameter's step by dividing the global learning rate by the square root of this running total. A parameter whose gradients have consistently been large builds up a big accumulator and so takes ever smaller steps; a parameter that has rarely seen a meaningful gradient keeps a small accumulator and so retains a large effective step. The single global rate is thereby reshaped into a personalized rate per parameter, set entirely by each parameter's own gradient history.

This per-parameter adaptation matters most for sparse data, which is the setting AdaGrad was designed for. In problems like text and recommendation, most features are zero in any given example, so some parameters are updated on almost every step while others fire only occasionally. A single shared learning rate forces a bad compromise: large enough to make progress on the rare features and it destabilizes the common ones, small enough to be safe on the common ones and the rare features never learn. AdaGrad dissolves the compromise by letting the rare, informative features keep big steps while the frequent ones are gently damped, and it delivered strong results on exactly these large, sparse machine-learning problems. It also removes much of the burden of hand-tuning a learning-rate schedule, since the per-parameter decay emerges automatically from the accumulators.

AdaGrad matters historically as the conceptual seed of the adaptive optimizers that dominate deep learning. Its core move, normalizing each parameter's step by a running measure of its squared gradients, is inherited directly by RMSprop and by Adam, both of which can be read as fixes to AdaGrad's central flaw. That flaw is its memory: because the accumulator sums squared gradients over all of training and only ever grows, the effective learning rate decreases monotonically toward zero. On convex problems this aggressive decay is well-justified and AdaGrad comes with strong regret guarantees, but in the long, non-convex training runs of deep networks the rate often shrinks to nothing before the model has finished learning, and training stalls prematurely.

RMSprop fixes this by replacing the ever-growing sum with an exponentially decaying moving average of squared gradients, so the accumulator forgets the distant past and the effective rate no longer collapses, and Adam adds momentum on top of that. AdaGrad is therefore rarely the optimizer of choice for training large modern networks, but it remains a clean, well-understood baseline, an effective choice for genuinely sparse convex problems, and the essential first step in the lineage that runs through RMSprop to Adam. Understanding why its accumulator decays the learning rate to zero is the clearest way to understand what the later optimizers were built to repair.
