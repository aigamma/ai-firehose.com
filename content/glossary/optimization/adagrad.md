AdaGrad, the adaptive gradient algorithm, was the first widely used optimizer to give every parameter its own learning rate rather than sharing one across the whole model. For each parameter separately it accumulates the sum of the squares of all the gradients it has ever received, and scales that parameter's step by dividing the global learning rate by the square root of this running total. A parameter whose gradients have consistently been large builds a big accumulator and takes ever smaller steps; one that rarely sees a meaningful gradient keeps a small accumulator and retains a large effective step. The single global rate is reshaped into a personalized rate per parameter, set entirely by each parameter's own gradient history.

This per-parameter adaptation matters most for sparse data, the setting AdaGrad was designed for. In problems like text and recommendation, most features are zero in any given example, so some parameters update on almost every step while others fire only occasionally, and a single shared rate forces a bad compromise: large enough to move the rare features and it destabilizes the common ones, small enough to be safe on the common ones and the rare ones never learn. AdaGrad dissolves the compromise, letting rare, informative features keep big steps while frequent ones are gently damped, and it delivered strong results on exactly these large, sparse problems while removing much of the burden of hand-tuning a schedule.

AdaGrad's lasting importance is as the conceptual seed of the adaptive optimizers that dominate deep learning. Its core move, normalizing each parameter's step by a running measure of its squared gradients, is inherited directly by RMSprop and by Adam, both of which can be read as fixes to AdaGrad's one central flaw. That flaw is its memory: because the accumulator sums squared gradients over all of training and only ever grows, the effective learning rate decreases monotonically toward zero. On convex problems this aggressive decay is well-justified and AdaGrad carries strong regret guarantees, but in the long, non-convex runs of deep networks the rate often shrinks to nothing before the model has finished learning, and training stalls prematurely.

RMSprop repairs this by replacing the ever-growing sum with an exponentially decaying moving average, so the accumulator forgets the distant past and the effective rate no longer collapses, and Adam adds momentum on top. AdaGrad is therefore rarely the optimizer of choice for large modern networks, but it remains a clean, well-understood baseline, a genuinely good choice for sparse convex problems, and the essential first step in the lineage that runs through RMSprop to Adam. Understanding why its accumulator decays the rate to zero is the single clearest way to understand what every adaptive optimizer after it was built to fix.
