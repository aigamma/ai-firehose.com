A logged dataset records what happened: this user was shown this ad and clicked, this patient got this treatment and recovered. The question you actually want answered is counterfactual: what would have happened under a different choice, a different ad, a different treatment, the one you did not make? Counterfactual learning is the discipline of squeezing that answer out of data about decisions already taken, so you can evaluate or improve a policy without the expense and risk of running it live.

The central obstacle is that the data only shows the chosen actions and their outcomes, never the alternatives, and the choices were not random: the old policy preferred certain actions, so they are overrepresented and the rest are barely seen. Naively learning from such logs bakes in the old policy's biases. The fix borrows from causal inference and statistics: reweight the logged outcomes by how likely the new policy would have been to take each action versus the old one, an importance-sampling correction that, in expectation, recovers what the new policy would have scored. The price is variance, which explodes when the two policies disagree sharply.

This is exactly the logic that makes off-policy reinforcement learning possible, learning the value of one policy from data generated by another, and it connects to inverse reinforcement learning, which counterfactually infers the reward that would explain observed behavior. In all of these, the move is the same: treat the log as a biased sample of a world of possible actions and correct for the bias rather than pretend it is not there.

Counterfactual learning matters because the alternative, deploying every candidate policy to see how it does, is often slow, costly, or unethical, you cannot A/B test a medical policy on real patients lightly. Being able to ask "what if we had done differently" from history alone is what lets recommenders, ad systems, and decision policies improve safely offline, and its honest limit is the same as all causal reasoning from observational data: the answer is only as trustworthy as the assumption that the log captured the relevant alternatives at all.
