Ask what could go wrong with AI and you get answers that barely belong in the same conversation: a biased hiring model, a flood of convincing disinformation, an automated cyberattack, and a superintelligence pursuing goals no human shares. AI risk is the umbrella over that whole spectrum, and most of the disagreement in the field is not about whether risks are real but about where on the spectrum to look, and how to weigh a likely small harm against an unlikely catastrophic one.

It helps to sort the risks along two axes. By timing: present harms (bias, fraud, surveillance, job displacement) that are happening now, against future harms (loss of control over highly capable systems) that are projected. And by cause: misuse, a human deliberately turning AI to harm, against accident, a system doing damage no one intended because its objective was subtly wrong. Where someone places their attention on these axes largely determines which camp they are in, and the camps often talk past each other because they are weighing genuinely different scenarios.

The hardest feature of AI risk is the inverse relation between a risk's probability and the cost of being wrong about it. The present harms are near-certain but bounded; the catastrophic ones are speculative but, if real, unrecoverable, and you cannot run the experiment to find out. This is why the debate is so heated and so unresolved: it is not a factual disagreement that evidence can settle but a judgment under deep uncertainty about how much to spend now to hedge a tail risk you cannot yet measure, the kind of decision humans are notoriously bad at making well.

What makes AI risk distinctive among technological risks is the combination of speed, autonomy, and opacity: systems that act faster than oversight, increasingly without a human in the loop, and whose reasoning we cannot fully inspect. That combination shrinks the margin for the ordinary way societies manage risk, which is to wait for harms to appear and then respond. The serious question underneath every AI-risk debate is therefore whether our institutions can adapt fast enough to govern a technology that does not wait for them, which is a question about us as much as about the machines.
