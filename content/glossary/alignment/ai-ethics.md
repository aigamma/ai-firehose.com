Most AI questions are about what a system can do; AI ethics asks what it should do, and what its builders and users owe to the people it affects. It is the normative layer under the technical and legal ones: where alignment asks how to make a model do what we want and regulation asks what to require by law, ethics asks the prior question of what we should want in the first place, and who counts as we.

The concerns cluster around a few values. Fairness: that a model not encode and amplify the biases in its training data against the people it judges. Accountability: that when an automated decision causes harm, someone is answerable, not just an inscrutable model. Transparency: that those affected can understand and contest how a system treats them. And power: that the ability to build and deploy these systems, concentrated in a few firms, not become an unaccountable concentration of influence over information, work, and opportunity.

The hardest problem in AI ethics is that there is no single human value to align to, because humans disagree, across cultures, interests, and the present versus the future. A model trained to be helpful, harmless, and honest still embeds choices about whose notion of harm, and a system that satisfies one group's values can violate another's. So AI ethics is not a checklist to be completed but a permanent negotiation, and its honest practitioners are wary of any claim to have solved it, since solving it would mean settling moral disagreements that predate the technology by millennia.

What gives AI ethics its urgency is scale and speed: a biased human decision affects the people in front of one person, but a biased model makes the same decision millions of times, instantly, with the false authority of a machine. The technology does not create new moral questions so much as it industrializes the old ones, which is why ethics cannot be a final review stage bolted onto a finished system but has to be built into the choices about what to build, and how, from the start.
