Of all the jobs AI might automate, the one with the strangest implications is AI research itself. AI research automation is the use of AI systems to do the work that produces better AI: proposing ideas, writing and running experiments, tuning models, and reading the literature. It is ordinary automation pointed at an extraordinary target, because the output of this particular job is more capable versions of the thing doing it.

The work of an AI researcher decomposes into tasks agents are increasingly able to attempt: implementing an architecture from a description, running and evaluating a sweep of experiments, summarizing results, generating the next hypothesis. None of these is science fiction; pieces of them already run in labs as tools that speed up the humans. The open question is how much of the loop can be closed, how far an automated researcher can go from idea to validated improvement without a person in the path.

This is exactly where the acceleration story and the safety story collide. To the optimist, automating research is the lever that could compress decades of progress into years, because the rate-limiting human step is removed. To the worried, it is the precise mechanism that turns recursive self-improvement from an abstraction into an engineering project, removing the human oversight that currently bounds how fast and how far capability can move. Both are describing the same capability; they disagree only on whether taking humans out of the research loop is the goal or the danger.

AI research automation is worth watching as the leading indicator for the most consequential scenarios, because it is the bridge between today's tool-using agents and the runaway-improvement futures that safety debates turn on. How carefully it is pursued, whether labs keep humans meaningfully in the loop and instrument the systems doing the research, is one of the more important and least visible choices being made in AI right now, precisely because it shapes whether the field stays something humans steer or becomes something that steers itself.
