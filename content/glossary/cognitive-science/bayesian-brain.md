The Bayesian brain hypothesis holds that perception, cognition, and action are fundamentally problems of statistical inference, and that the brain solves them in something close to the optimal Bayesian way. The senses deliver noisy, ambiguous, incomplete data, and the world that produced them is hidden. To recover the likely causes of its sensations, the brain is said to maintain probability distributions over those causes and to combine two ingredients in the manner of bayes-theorem: a prior, encoding expectations learned from past experience, and a likelihood, encoding how well each possible cause explains the current input. The result is a posterior belief that is then read out as perception or used to guide behavior.

The lineage reaches back to Hermann von Helmholtz, who in the nineteenth century described perception as unconscious inference, and it was revived in computational form through the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries by researchers including David Knill, Alexandre Pouget, and Karl Friston. The hypothesis earns its keep empirically because it predicts a precise way the brain should weigh different signals, the keeper: each cue should be trusted in proportion to its reliability. Human cue-integration experiments bear this out strikingly. When people combine vision and touch, or two visual cues, they weight each almost exactly by its inverse variance, the statistically optimal rule, which is hard to explain unless the brain is tracking the uncertainty of its information.

The hypothesis is best understood as a level of description that other theories implement. Predictive-coding is the leading proposal for the mechanism: a hierarchy that encodes predictions and passes prediction errors is a concrete neural scheme for carrying out Bayesian updating, with the brain's confidence in a signal, its precision, playing the role of inverse variance. The free-energy-principle generalizes this further, arguing that minimizing variational free energy just is approximate Bayesian inference, so perception and action both fall under one optimization. A standing puzzle is neural-coding: how populations of spiking neurons actually represent full probability-distributions rather than single best guesses, with proposals ranging from probabilistic population codes to sampling schemes in which neural variability is the brain drawing samples from a posterior.

For artificial intelligence the Bayesian brain is both an inspiration and a benchmark. It frames the goal of perception as inferring a generative model's latent causes, exactly the objective of the variational-autoencoder and other deep generative models, and it explains illusions and context effects as the rational consequence of strong priors meeting weak evidence rather than as bugs. It also sets a high bar that current systems often miss: a Bayesian agent represents and respects its own uncertainty, whereas a standard neural-network tends to be confidently wrong off its training distribution. Much of the push toward calibrated, uncertainty-aware, and robust machine learning is, in effect, an effort to make artificial systems more like the brain the hypothesis describes.
