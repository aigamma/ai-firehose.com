Global workspace theory explains consciousness as a matter of broadcast. The brain runs a vast collection of specialized, unconscious processors in parallel, each handling vision, language, memory, motor control, and so on. Most of their activity stays local and never reaches awareness. But these processors compete for entry into a limited-capacity central resource, the global workspace, and the content that wins access is broadcast widely back to the whole system, where it becomes consciously experienced and simultaneously available to every other processor. The metaphor is a theater: a spotlight of attention illuminates one item on a stage, and the lit content is shared with the audience of background processes.

The theory was introduced by the cognitive psychologist Bernard Baars in the late 1980s and was later given a neural grounding by Stanislas Dehaene and Jean-Pierre Changeux as the global neuronal workspace, which identifies the workspace with long-range connections among prefrontal and parietal cortex. Their work tied the theory to a measurable signature: a stimulus that crosses the threshold into consciousness triggers a sudden, late, brain-wide ignition of coordinated activity, whereas an unconscious stimulus produces only local, transient processing. This makes the theory unusually empirical for a theory of consciousness, with specific predictions about what distinguishes a perceived from an unperceived signal.

Several familiar ideas slot into this picture. The limited capacity of the workspace is closely related to working-memory and to the bottleneck of attention, explaining why we can consciously juggle only a few items at once even though the brain processes enormously in parallel. The theory also harmonizes with dual-process-theory accounts of cognition: fast, automatic, unconscious computation corresponds to the parallel specialists, while slow, effortful, serial reasoning corresponds to operations carried out on content held in the workspace. Consciousness, in this view, is not a luxury but a functional device for routing information flexibly among modules that would otherwise stay isolated.

The bridge to artificial intelligence has grown stronger as architectures have scaled. Global workspace theory is essentially a claim about a shared communication bottleneck through which specialized modules exchange information, and that is structurally close to the attention mechanism at the heart of the transformer, which lets every part of a model selectively read from a common pool of representations. Researchers have proposed explicit global-workspace modules for deep learning, in which competing neural-network components write to and read from a shared, capacity-limited blackboard to coordinate. The theory also anchors serious discussion of machine consciousness, offering a functional checklist, broadcast, ignition, global availability, that one can in principle ask whether an AI system implements, independent of whether it has subjective experience.
