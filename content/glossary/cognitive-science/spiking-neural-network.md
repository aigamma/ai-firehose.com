A conventional neural-network neuron outputs a continuous number on every forward pass; a biological neuron mostly stays silent and speaks in discrete, time-stamped spikes. A spiking neural network is built to model that second picture: its neurons communicate through spikes that occur at specific moments, each one integrating its incoming spikes over time, raising an internal membrane potential, and emitting a spike of its own only when that potential crosses a threshold, after which it resets. The leaky integrate-and-fire neuron is the standard simple model, and richer variants like the Hodgkin-Huxley and Izhikevich models trade simplicity for biological detail. Because the timing of each spike carries information, time is a first-class dimension of computation rather than an afterthought.

This temporal character connects spiking networks tightly to neural-coding, the question of how meaning is carried in spike activity. A spiking network can exploit rate codes, where the number of spikes in a window matters, or temporal codes, where the precise timing and order of spikes matter, and it can represent information sparsely because a neuron that has nothing to say simply stays silent. Spikes are often described as the third generation of neural network models, following the threshold units of the perceptron era and the continuous-valued networks of modern deep learning, with the distinguishing feature being explicit, event-driven temporal dynamics.

Training spiking networks is the central difficulty. The spike is a discontinuous, all-or-nothing event, so its output is not differentiable, which blocks the direct use of backpropagation that trains ordinary deep networks. Researchers work around this in several ways: surrogate-gradient methods smooth the spike with a differentiable approximation during the backward pass, conversion methods train a standard network and then map it onto a spiking one, and biologically grounded approaches lean on local rules such as hebbian-learning and spike-timing-dependent plasticity that need no global error signal at all. Each route reflects a different bet about whether to import deep learning's machinery or to honor the brain's locality constraints.

The payoff for this added complexity is efficiency and realism. Because computation is event-driven, a spiking network does work only when spikes occur, which on the right substrate translates into very low power consumption. That substrate is neuromorphic-computing hardware, such as Intel's Loihi and IBM's TrueNorth chips, which are physically built to run spiking dynamics in parallel with energy budgets far below those of GPUs. Spiking networks are therefore the natural software layer for brain-inspired chips, and a leading approach for always-on sensing, robotics, and edge AI where battery life and latency matter more than squeezing out the last point of benchmark accuracy.
