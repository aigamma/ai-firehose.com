Neuromorphic computing designs hardware in the image of the brain, and the keeper is what it rejects. Conventional computers follow the von Neumann architecture, in which a processor and a separate memory shuttle data back and forth across a bus, a movement that dominates the energy budget of modern AI. Neuromorphic systems reject that separation: they colocate memory and computation in dense networks of artificial neurons and synapses, communicate through sparse, asynchronous spikes rather than a global clock, and do work only when there is activity. The goal is not to run today's algorithms faster but to match the brain's remarkable efficiency, roughly twenty watts for a system no transistor count has yet rivaled in general intelligence.

The field was named and founded by Carver Mead at Caltech in the late 1980s, who built analog circuits, silicon retinas and cochleas, whose transistors operated in a regime where their physics directly emulated the behavior of ion channels in neurons. That founding insight, that you can let the physics of the device do the computation rather than digitizing everything, still animates the field. The natural software for these machines is the spiking-neural-network, because event-driven spiking hardware and spike-based models are built for each other, and neural-coding questions about rate versus timing become concrete engineering choices in silicon.

A defining ambition of neuromorphic hardware is on-chip learning. Because memory and computation sit together, a synapse can update its own weight from locally available signals without a separate training phase on another machine. This makes local rules such as hebbian-learning and spike-timing-dependent plasticity a natural fit, since they need only the activity of the neurons a synapse connects, not the global backward pass that backpropagation requires. Emerging device technologies, especially the memristor, promise synapses whose conductance physically stores a weight and changes with use, collapsing storage and adaptation into a single component.

Concrete platforms have moved the field from theory to silicon: IBM's TrueNorth, Intel's Loihi, the University of Manchester's SpiNNaker, and Heidelberg's BrainScaleS each realize large spiking systems with very different trade-offs of analog versus digital and speed versus fidelity. Their strengths are clearest where power and latency dominate, including always-on sensors, robotics, and edge AI that must learn and react without a data-center connection. The persistent challenges are a less mature programming model and a training story still catching up to gradient-based deep learning. Even so, as the energy cost of large neural-network models keeps rising, neuromorphic computing stands as one of the most serious bets that the path to sustainable, embodied AI runs through hardware that computes the way brains do.
