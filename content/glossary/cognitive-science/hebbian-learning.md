Hebbian learning is the foundational principle of synaptic plasticity, summarized in the slogan that neurons which fire together wire together. It was proposed by the psychologist Donald Hebb in his 1949 book The Organization of Behavior, which postulated that when an axon of cell A is near enough to excite cell B and repeatedly takes part in firing it, some growth process increases the efficiency of A's influence on B. In modern terms, the weight of a synapse grows in proportion to the correlated activity of the neurons it connects. Knowledge is stored in these strengthened connections, an idea that became the cornerstone of connectionism.

The rule's defining virtue is that it is local and unsupervised, the keeper. A synapse adjusts using only the activity of the two neurons it joins, with no teacher signal and no information about distant errors elsewhere in the network. This is biologically realistic in a way that the global error propagation of backpropagation is not, since a real synapse has no access to a network-wide loss. The cost is that pure Hebbian strengthening is unstable: weights that only ever grow will run away without bound, so practical versions add normalization or decay. Oja's rule and the BCM theory are classic stabilized variants, and they reveal that Hebbian dynamics naturally extract the principal components of their input, linking the rule to unsupervised feature discovery.

Decades after Hebb, neuroscience gave the principle a sharper empirical form. Long-term potentiation, the lasting strengthening of synapses after correlated stimulation, is widely regarded as a physical substrate of Hebbian learning, and spike-timing-dependent plasticity refined the timing: a synapse strengthens when the presynaptic spike precedes the postsynaptic one and weakens when the order reverses, encoding causality rather than mere coincidence. This timing sensitivity is what makes Hebbian rules a natural fit for the spiking-neural-network, where information lives in precise spike times rather than continuous activation values.

In artificial intelligence, Hebbian learning is both a historical root and a live alternative. It powered early associative memories such as the Hopfield network, where Hebbian-imprinted patterns become stable attractors the network can recall from partial cues, and it underlies competitive learning schemes like self-organizing maps. Today it draws renewed interest as researchers seek training methods that are more biologically plausible and more hardware-friendly than backpropagation, especially for neuromorphic-computing chips that learn on-device. Local Hebbian updates sidestep the need to store activations and run a separate backward pass, making them attractive for low-power, continuously adapting systems even where they do not yet match the raw accuracy of gradient-trained networks.
