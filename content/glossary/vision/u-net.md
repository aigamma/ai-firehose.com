A U-Net is a convolutional encoder-decoder whose shape, drawn as a diagram, looks like the letter U: a contracting path that downsamples the image into compact, abstract features, then a symmetric expanding path that upsamples those features back to the original resolution. Its signature is the set of skip connections bridging the two halves at matching resolutions, copying the high-detail feature maps from each encoder stage directly across to the corresponding decoder stage. Introduced in 2015 for biomedical image segmentation, it has become one of the most reused architectures in all of deep learning.

The skip connections are the whole trick, and they solve a specific problem. Downsampling builds features that know what is in the image but throw away precisely where, because pooling and strided convolution discard spatial resolution, and upsampling alone would then have to hallucinate the lost boundaries, producing blurry results. By carrying the early, high-resolution feature maps across the U and concatenating them with the upsampled features, the decoder gets both the abstract semantic content from deep in the network and the crisp spatial detail from near the input, so it can place sharp edges exactly where they belong. This is why the U-Net became the template for semantic segmentation and instance segmentation, where the boundary itself is the answer and a few pixels of error matter.

Mechanically the architecture is a stack of convolution blocks. The encoder alternates convolutions with downsampling so the spatial size halves and the channel count grows at each stage; the decoder alternates upsampling with convolutions so the spatial size doubles back while the skip-connected detail is fused in. Because each decoder stage receives a skip tensor from the encoder, the design is closely related in spirit to a residual connection: both create short paths that let information and gradients bypass the deep bottleneck, stabilizing training and preserving detail. The result is a network that maps an image to a same-size output, which makes it natural for any dense-prediction task, segmentation, depth estimation, image restoration, and translation between image domains.

Beyond its origins in vision, the U-Net found a second, even larger life as the denoising network at the heart of the diffusion model. A diffusion model is trained to predict the noise in a corrupted image, which is itself a dense, image-to-image regression, exactly the shape a U-Net excels at, so most image diffusion systems, including the latent diffusion models behind popular generators, use a U-Net augmented with attention layers and timestep conditioning as their backbone, with text conditioning injected through cross-attention at several resolutions. Only recently has the transformer-based diffusion backbone begun to displace it at large scale, but the U-Net remains the default and the conceptual reference point for dense prediction.
