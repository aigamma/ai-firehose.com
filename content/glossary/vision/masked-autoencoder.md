A masked autoencoder, abbreviated MAE, learns to see by learning to fill in the blanks. It takes an image, hides a large fraction of its patches, and trains a model to reconstruct the pixels that were removed from the small remainder that was kept. Because the target is just the image's own missing content, no human labels are needed, which makes it a form of self-supervised learning, and the method, introduced for vision in 2021, adapts the masked-prediction idea that had already transformed language pretraining into the image domain by predicting masked-out patches rather than masked-out words.

It matters because labels are the expensive bottleneck in computer vision, and masked autoencoding turns the vast supply of unlabeled images into useful pretraining signal. A model pretrained this way learns general visual features, edges, textures, parts, and the way they compose, that transfer to downstream tasks like classification, detection, and segmentation after a light fine-tuning on a much smaller labeled set. The reconstruction objective forces the model to build an internal model of how images are structured, since it can only guess the hidden regions by understanding the visible ones, which is what produces representations that generalize rather than memorize.

The design hinges on two choices that make it both effective and efficient. First, the masking ratio is deliberately very high, often around seventy-five percent of patches removed, far higher than the masking used in language; an image is so spatially redundant that hiding a little would let the model cheat by copying nearby pixels, so a large hole forces genuine understanding. Second, the architecture is an asymmetric encoder-decoder: a heavy vision transformer encoder processes only the small set of visible patches, ignoring the masked positions entirely, while a lightweight decoder takes the encoded visible patches plus placeholder tokens for the missing ones and reconstructs the full image. Processing only the visible quarter makes the expensive encoder several times cheaper to pretrain, a large practical advantage, and the patch grid that makes this possible is the same image tokenization step the vision transformer relies on.

Masked autoencoding is one of the two dominant families of self-supervised visual pretraining, and the contrast with the other is instructive. Contrastive learning teaches representations by pulling together different augmented views of the same image and pushing apart views of different images, learning what makes images similar or distinct; masked autoencoding instead learns by reconstruction, with no need for carefully designed augmentations or large batches of negatives. The reconstructive approach tends to produce features that fine-tune especially well, and the masking-and-reconstruction principle generalizes cleanly across modalities, with the same recipe applied to audio spectrograms and video. It is closely related to the general autoencoder idea of compressing and reconstructing data, specialized to learn transferable features by reconstructing only what was hidden.
