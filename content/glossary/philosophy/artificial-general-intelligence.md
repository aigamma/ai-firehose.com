Artificial general intelligence (AGI) denotes a system whose intelligence is general in the way human intelligence is: able to learn, reason, plan, and solve problems across an open-ended range of domains, transferring knowledge from one area to another and handling novel situations it was never specifically trained for. It is defined by contrast with narrow AI, the systems that dominate practice, which are superb within a circumscribed task, playing Go, recognizing faces, predicting the next token, but do not carry that competence flexibly into unrelated problems. The phrase "artificial general intelligence" was popularized in the early 2000s (often credited to discussions involving Shane Legg, Ben Goertzel, and others), but the aspiration is as old as the field; it is close to what the 1956 Dartmouth proposal meant by simulating "every aspect" of intelligence, and to what John Searle later labeled Strong AI, the thesis that an appropriately programmed machine would genuinely have a mind.

A persistent difficulty is that AGI has no agreed definition or test, because "general intelligence" is itself contested. The Turing Test, proposed by Alan Turing in 1950, operationalizes intelligence as indistinguishability from a human in conversation, but it is widely seen as both too easy, gameable by systems that mimic without understanding, and beside the point, since it measures imitation rather than the underlying capacity. Proposed alternatives include batteries of diverse tasks, measures of sample-efficient learning and transfer, and formal definitions such as Legg and Marcus Hutter's, which characterizes intelligence as an agent's expected performance across a broad space of environments. Each captures something and misses something, and the lack of a crisp target is part of why claims that AGI is near or far so often talk past one another. The arrival of large language models that display broad, surprising competence has intensified the dispute over whether generality is being approached or merely imitated.

The prospect of AGI is where the conceptual entries in this glossary converge, because whether it is possible at all turns on positions in the philosophy of mind. If functionalism and the computational-theory-of-mind are correct, then general intelligence is substrate-independent and building it is an engineering problem with no in-principle barrier, since the right organization can run on non-biological hardware. Searle's chinese-room argument and the symbol-grounding-problem press the contrary worry that formal computation, however general its behavior, may yield mimicry without genuine understanding or intentionality. Advocates of embodied cognition argue that general intelligence may require a body and active engagement with a world, not text and computation alone. And the question of whether broad new competence could simply emerge from scaling computation connects AGI directly to debates about emergence. Notably, generality of intelligence is a separate question from consciousness: a system could in principle be fully general yet, if the hard-problem-of-consciousness is real, have no inner experience, and might in that respect be a kind of philosophical-zombie.

AGI matters because it is the horizon against which present AI is measured and the stake that gives the philosophy of mind contemporary urgency, while also raising distinct practical concerns about safety, alignment, and economic and social disruption that do not depend on resolving the metaphysics. Forecasts range from imminent to impossible, and the disagreement is rarely just empirical; it reflects different underlying commitments about what intelligence is and whether the methods now scaling so impressively are on a path to generality or are sophisticated pattern-matchers approaching their ceiling. Clarifying AGI therefore requires clarifying the concepts it rests on, which is why the term sits at the center of this category rather than at its edge, and why serious thinking about the future of AI is inseparable from serious thinking about the nature of mind.
