Functionalism is the thesis that what makes something a mental state is the role it plays, not the stuff it is made of. A state counts as pain, on this view, because of its causal profile: it is typically caused by bodily damage, it causes wincing and avoidance, and it interacts with beliefs and desires in characteristic ways. Anything that occupies that role is pain, whether the underlying medium is carbon neurons, silicon circuits, or something else. The position was developed in the 1960s and 1970s, with Hilary Putnam its pivotal early architect, alongside contributions from figures such as Jerry Fodor and David Lewis. It arose as a correction to two predecessors: behaviorism, which tried to define mental states purely by outward behavior and ignored inner states, and the mind-brain identity theory, which tied each mental state to a specific physical brain state.

Putnam's central argument is multiple realizability. The same mental state can be realized in radically different physical systems. Humans, octopuses, and hypothetical silicon-based aliens might all feel pain despite sharing no relevant brain structure, so pain cannot be identical to any particular physical state like the firing of human C-fibers. What they share is the functional organization, the pattern of causal relations, abstracted away from the hardware. This abstraction is exactly the move that makes minds, in principle, substrate-independent, and it is why functionalism became the implicit philosophy of much of artificial intelligence and cognitive science. If mental states are functional roles, then building a mind is a matter of instantiating the right organization in whatever medium is convenient. The computational-theory-of-mind is the most influential species of functionalism, identifying the relevant roles with computational ones.

Functionalism faces two famous objections, both aimed at whether function exhausts the mind. The first is the absent-qualia worry, sharpened by the philosophical-zombie thought experiment and by Ned Block's "China Brain" (or "Chinese Nation") case, in which the entire population of a country, passing signals by radio, jointly realizes the functional organization of a mind. Intuitively the resulting system has no subjective experience, suggesting that functional organization alone does not guarantee qualia. The second is the inverted-qualia worry: two people could share identical functional organization yet have systematically swapped color experiences, your red where they see green, with no functional difference to mark it, which suggests that the felt character of experience floats free of function. Searle's chinese-room argument presses a parallel point about understanding and intentionality rather than experience: that the right functional input-output profile can be present while genuine meaning is absent. These objections converge on the hard-problem-of-consciousness, the claim that there is an aspect of mind, its felt quality, that no functional account seems to capture.

Functionalism matters for AI because it supplies the principled reason to think machine minds are possible at all. If it is correct, then nothing about being made of metal and electricity bars a system from having beliefs, desires, or understanding, provided it instantiates the right causal-organizational structure, and the path to artificial-general-intelligence is in principle open. If the objections succeed, then even a perfect functional duplicate of a human mind might lack the very things we most care about, consciousness and the felt presence of meaning, and the engineering project would leave the deepest questions untouched. Most working AI proceeds on functionalist assumptions, often without naming them; making those assumptions explicit clarifies both what success would and would not show.
