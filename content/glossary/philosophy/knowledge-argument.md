The knowledge argument is a famous challenge to physicalism, the thesis that everything that exists is physical and that all facts are, at bottom, physical facts. It was given its canonical form by Frank Jackson in his 1982 paper "Epiphenomenal Qualia" and refined in "What Mary Didn't Know" (1986). The argument is built around a thought experiment usually called Mary's Room. Mary is a brilliant scientist who has spent her entire life in a black-and-white room, learning about the world through black-and-white books and a monochrome monitor. She comes to know every physical fact there is about color vision: the wavelengths of light, the behavior of cone cells, the neural pathways, the complete physics and neuroscience of how human beings see and process color. By stipulation she knows all the physical facts. Then one day she leaves the room and sees a ripe tomato, experiencing red for the first time.

The argument turns on a single intuitive question: does Mary learn anything new when she first sees red? It seems obvious that she does. She learns what it is like to see red, the felt quality of the experience, which no amount of black-and-white physical knowledge had given her. But if she already knew all the physical facts and yet learns something new, then what she learns is a fact that is not physical: there are non-physical facts about experience, namely facts about its qualitative character, its qualia. Therefore physicalism is false: a complete physical description of the world leaves something out, the subjective feel of conscious states. Compactly, the premises are that Mary knows all the physical facts before release and that she gains new knowledge on release, from which it follows that not all knowledge is physical knowledge, so not all facts are physical facts.

The knowledge argument has drawn a large and ingenious body of replies, and the dispute is far from settled. The most influential physicalist response is the ability hypothesis, defended by Laurence Nemirow and David Lewis: Mary gains no new propositional fact but only new abilities, the know-how to recognize, imagine, and remember red, so what she acquires is a skill rather than a fact, and physicalism is untouched. A second major line is the old fact, new mode of presentation reply: Mary learns an old physical fact under a new, first-personal phenomenal concept, much as one might learn that the morning star is the evening star without learning a new object, so there is a new way of grasping a fact but no new fact. Other physicalists challenge the premise itself, arguing that no one could truly know all the physical facts about color and still be surprised, or that the intuition trades on conflating two senses of knowing. Tellingly, Jackson himself later abandoned the argument, coming to think the new-knowledge intuition can be explained within physicalism.

For AI and the philosophy of mind, the knowledge argument sharpens the same worry the hard-problem-of-consciousness and the philosophical-zombie press from other directions: that complete objective, third-person knowledge of a system may not capture the first-person, qualitative side of mind. If the argument succeeds, then a machine could in principle possess and process every physical and computational fact about color, or about any experience, while it remains an open question whether there is anything it is like to be that machine. Full physical and functional description, the only kind of description an engineer or an external observer can give, would by hypothesis leave the felt character undetermined. This is why the knowledge argument bears directly on debates over machine consciousness and over the limits of functionalism and the computational-theory-of-mind: it claims that knowing everything about the mechanism is compatible with missing the experience, which means no amount of capability, however complete, would automatically settle whether an artificial system feels anything at all.
