Intentionality is the philosophers' term for aboutness: the feature of mental states by which they are directed at, refer to, or represent objects and states of affairs. A belief is always a belief that something is the case; a desire is a desire for something; a fear is a fear of something. The thought is about the Eiffel Tower in a way that no rock or puddle is about anything. The term comes from the Latin intendere, to aim at, and despite the spelling it has nothing specifically to do with intending or purpose. The notion descends from medieval scholastic philosophy and was reintroduced to modern thought by Franz Brentano in his 1874 work "Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint", where he proposed intentionality as the mark of the mental, the characteristic that distinguishes mental phenomena from physical ones.

A puzzle that has shaped the debate is that intentional states can be directed at things that do not exist. One can hope for world peace that never comes, believe in a planet that turns out not to be there, or think about a unicorn. This "intentional inexistence" makes aboutness hard to model as an ordinary physical relation, since a genuine relation normally requires both relata to exist. A second puzzle is that intentional contexts are referentially opaque: someone can believe the Morning Star is bright without believing the Evening Star is bright, even though both are the planet Venus, so the content of a thought is fine-grained in a way mere reference to objects is not. Naturalizing intentionality, explaining aboutness in physical, scientifically respectable terms, became a central project of late-twentieth-century philosophy of mind, pursued through causal theories, teleological theories that ground content in evolutionary function, and the representationalism of the computational-theory-of-mind.

A distinction due in its modern form to John Searle is pivotal for AI, the keeper: original (or intrinsic) intentionality versus derived intentionality. The words on this page are about things, but only derivatively; their aboutness is borrowed, conferred by the minds of writers and readers who interpret them. A map represents terrain only because we read it that way. Searle argues that the symbols inside a computer have at most derived intentionality, meaning lent by the programmers and users who interpret the outputs, never the original intentionality that minds possess on their own account. This is the heart of his chinese-room argument and is tightly bound to the symbol-grounding-problem: the question of how, if ever, a system's internal states could come to be about the world intrinsically rather than only in the eyes of an outside interpreter. Defenders of strong AI reply that original intentionality is itself a natural phenomenon, arising from the right causal and functional relations between a system and its environment, in which case a sufficiently embodied and connected machine could have it too.

Intentionality matters for AI because it is the philosophical name for the difference between a system that genuinely refers to the world and one that merely emits symbols we find it convenient to interpret as referring. A large language model produces sentences that are, on their surface, richly about the world, yet whether that aboutness is original or merely derived from the human-generated text it was trained on is exactly the contested question. Approaches in embodied cognition and multimodal learning can be read as attempts to give machines original intentionality by tying their representations to perception and action rather than to text alone. The concept thus sits at the crossroads of several entries here, and any serious claim that a machine "understands" or "knows" is ultimately a claim about whether it has intentionality of the original kind.
