LLM-as-judge is the practice of enlisting a capable language model to evaluate generated text, standing in for a human rater. Instead of comparing an output to a stored answer, the judge model reads the prompt and the response and renders a verdict: a numeric score against a rubric, a pass-or-fail on a criterion, or a preference between two candidate answers. It is the dominant way to evaluate open-ended generation, summarization, dialogue, and agentic behavior, where the qualities that matter, helpfulness, coherence, faithfulness, tone, resist the fixed-key scoring that benchmarks like mmlu rely on.

It matters because it broke a scaling bottleneck. Human evaluation is the gold standard for open-ended quality but is slow, costly, and inconsistent across raters, which throttled how fast teams could iterate. A judge model is fast, cheap, and reproducible, so an entire evaluation suite can be re-run on every code change. Studies have found that a strong judge agrees with human preference at rates comparable to the agreement between two humans, which is what made the technique credible enough to become standard in model development pipelines and public leaderboards. It also rescues metrics that overlap scores like rouge handle poorly, because a judge can credit a correct paraphrase and penalize a fluent falsehood that lexical overlap would miss.

In practice there are two main protocols. Pointwise scoring hands the judge one response plus a rubric and asks for a rating, often with a chain-of-thought rationale to improve reliability. Pairwise comparison shows the judge two responses to the same prompt and asks which is better, which tends to be more robust because relative judgments are easier and more stable than absolute ones; the resulting win-loss records are then aggregated, frequently into an elo-rating or a Bradley-Terry fit, to produce a leaderboard. Good harnesses pin the judge model and prompt, swap the order of the two candidates and average to cancel position bias, and report agreement with a human-labeled subset as a sanity check.

The limitations are serious and well studied, and the bias catalog is the keeper. Judge models carry biases: a position bias toward whichever answer is shown first, a verbosity bias toward longer answers, a self-preference bias toward outputs that resemble their own style, and a sycophancy bias toward confident, agreeable phrasing, all of which can reward the wrong thing. A judge can be fooled by fluent, authoritative prose that is factually wrong, since it has no independent ground truth, and it inherits the knowledge gaps and miscalibration (see calibration) of the underlying model. Using the same model family as both contestant and judge invites circularity, and because judge prompts are public, they are themselves exposed to the same gaming and benchmark-contamination pressures as any other benchmark.

LLM-as-judge is the scoring engine behind most modern open-ended evaluation, the mechanism that feeds head-to-head verdicts into an elo-rating leaderboard and the semantic successor to overlap metrics like rouge and bleu-score. It is strongest where the answer is a matter of quality rather than a checkable fact, and weakest exactly where verification is possible, in which case executable checks such as pass-at-k give a more trustworthy signal than any judge.
