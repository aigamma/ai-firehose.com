Optimal transport asks a concrete question with deep consequences: if one probability distribution says where mass currently sits and another says where you want it, what is the cheapest plan to rearrange the first into the second? The cost is set by how far each unit of mass must travel, usually a power of the ground distance. Gaspard Monge posed it in 1781 in terms of moving piles of earth, which is why a discrete version is still the earth mover's distance, and Leonid Kantorovich gave it its modern, tractable form in the 1940s by allowing mass to be split, turning a hard combinatorial assignment into a linear program over transport plans, joint distributions whose marginals are the two given ones.

The minimal cost is itself a distance between distributions, the Wasserstein distance, and what makes it special is that it respects the geometry of the underlying space. Two distributions on nearby but non-overlapping supports are judged close, because moving the mass a short way is cheap, whereas a divergence like KL divergence sees no overlap and blows up to infinity. This geometric sensitivity is exactly what pointwise comparisons lack, and it is why the Wasserstein distance yields meaningful gradients even when two distributions do not share support, the norm rather than the exception in high dimensions.

That single property reshaped generative modeling. The original generative adversarial network minimized a divergence that gave vanishing or unstable gradients when the generated and real distributions barely overlapped, precisely the early-training regime, and the Wasserstein GAN replaced it with the Wasserstein distance, using the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality that rewrites the distance as a maximization over one-Lipschitz functions a network can approximate. Training grew markedly more stable and the loss began to correlate with sample quality. The same distance grades generative fidelity broadly and supplies the geometric backbone for several diffusion and flow formulations. Solving the linear program exactly is costly, so practitioners add an entropic regularizer and run the Sinkhorn algorithm, a fast matrix-scaling iteration that yields a smoothed, differentiable distance fit to drop inside a training loop.

Beyond comparing distributions, optimal transport puts a geometry on the space of distributions itself. The Wasserstein distance turns that space into a metric space whose shortest paths, the displacement interpolations, are geodesics that morph one distribution into another by sliding mass along straight lines rather than fading one out while the other fades in. This Wasserstein geometry connects to gradient flows, where many physical diffusions are steepest descent of an energy in Wasserstein space, to domain adaptation by transporting a source distribution onto a target, and to the broader effort to do calculus directly on distributions. It is one of the cleanest bridges between measure theory, geometry, and modern generative machine learning.
