The Fourier transform rewrites a signal as a combination of pure waves. Any reasonable function of time or space can be decomposed into sinusoids of different frequencies, each weighted by a complex amplitude recording how strongly that frequency is present and with what phase. The transform maps the original signal, viewed in the time or space domain, to this profile of amplitudes across frequency, and an inverse transform reconstructs it exactly. A musical chord viewed as a pressure wave over time looks complicated; viewed in the frequency domain it is just a few sharp spikes at the constituent notes. That change of viewpoint, from how a signal evolves to what it is made of, is the entire idea.

What makes the transform more than a clever change of coordinates is the convolution theorem: convolution in one domain is plain pointwise multiplication in the other. Filtering, smoothing, and the sliding-window correlations expensive to compute directly become elementwise products in the frequency domain, which is why so much of signal processing is done there. The Fast Fourier Transform computes the discrete version in time proportional to n log n rather than n squared, a speedup so consequential that it ranks among the most important algorithms ever written, enabling real-time audio, image, and communications processing across all of engineering.

There is a clean linear-algebra reason the transform is so canonical: the sinusoids are the eigenvectors of any system invariant to shifts, so the Fourier transform is the spectral decomposition that simultaneously diagonalizes every shift-invariant convolution operator. Feed a pure frequency through such a system and you get back the same frequency, only rescaled, and that scaling is the system's frequency response. Seen through group theory, the transform is harmonic analysis on the translation group, decomposing signals into that group's irreducible representations, which is also why generalizations exist, replacing translation with the rotation group to get the spherical harmonics behind spherical convolutions and steerable filters.

In machine learning the Fourier transform recurs in several roles. Because convolution is multiplication in the frequency domain, the convolution at the heart of a convolutional neural network can be analyzed and sometimes computed spectrally, and a network is often understood through which frequencies it preserves or suppresses, the lens that explains spectral bias, the tendency of networks to fit low frequencies first. Fourier features, which lift inputs through sines and cosines at many frequencies, let models represent the high-frequency detail raw coordinates struggle to capture, a trick central to coordinate-based networks and close in spirit to the sinusoidal positional encoding that injects order into a transformer.

More abstractly, the Fourier transform is a change of basis in an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, swapping the basis of point-localized impulses for the basis of perfectly periodic waves, two complementary descriptions of one object joined by an exact, invertible, energy-preserving map. The deep tradeoff it exposes, that a signal cannot be arbitrarily sharp in both time and frequency at once, is the uncertainty principle, the same mathematics that governs quantum measurement. For an engineer the practical summary is simpler: when a problem involves periodicity, convolution, or shift-invariance, moving to the frequency domain usually makes the structure obvious and the computation cheap.
