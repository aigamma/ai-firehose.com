Group theory is the mathematics of symmetry, made precise. A group is a set of elements with a way to combine any two that is associative, has an identity that does nothing, and gives every element an inverse that undoes it. The canonical examples are collections of transformations: the rotations of a square, the permutations of a deck, the translations of the plane, the invertible matrices of a given size. In each the group law is composition, do one transformation then another, the identity is leaving things alone, and the inverse is reversing. A group is therefore an exact account of what it means for a system to have symmetry: the transformations that leave some essential structure unchanged are exactly the elements of a group.

The most useful single idea is the group action, the formal statement that a group operates on a space by transforming its points while respecting composition. An action partitions a space into orbits, the points reachable from one another, and assigns each point a stabilizer, the elements that fix it, and the relation between orbits, stabilizers, and the group's size gives the subject its first powerful counting results. Representation theory pushes further by realizing abstract group elements as concrete matrices acting on a vector space, so symmetry can be studied with linear algebra, and decomposing a representation into irreducible pieces is the algebraic heart of the Fourier transform and of spectral decomposition on symmetric domains.

Group theory matters in machine learning because the right inductive bias is very often a symmetry: if a task is unchanged when its input is transformed by some group, the model should be too, and building that in directly is far more efficient than hoping the network learns it from data. The two precise versions are invariance, where the output does not change when the input is transformed, and equivariance, where the output transforms in lockstep. The convolutional neural network is the famous case: convolution is exactly the operation equivariant to translation, which is why a detector that fires on an object in one corner of an image fires on it anywhere, and pooling then converts that equivariance into approximate invariance.

Geometric deep learning generalizes the insight by treating the choice of architecture as the choice of a symmetry group. If the data lives on a sphere, a graph, or a set, you ask which transformations should leave the answer unchanged, rotations, node permutations, set permutations, and build layers equivariant to that group by construction. Permutation-equivariant networks underpin set and graph models, and the self-attention mechanism is permutation-equivariant over its tokens, which is precisely why positional information has to be added back in separately. When the symmetries form a continuum rather than a discrete list, as with all rotations, the relevant object is a Lie group, which fuses group structure with the smooth geometry of differential geometry. Across the discrete and continuous cases the philosophy is one: find the transformations under which the problem is invariant, recognize they form a group, and use it to constrain the model.
