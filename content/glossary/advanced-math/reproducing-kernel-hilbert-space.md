A reproducing kernel Hilbert space, or RKHS, is a Hilbert space of functions in which evaluating a function at a point is a well-behaved operation rather than an ill-mannered one. In a generic space of functions, the value at a single point can be discontinuous and badly behaved; in an RKHS it is not, because for every input point there is a particular function in the space, the kernel section there, such that evaluating any function at that point equals its inner product with that section. This is the reproducing property: the inner product reproduces function values. The two-argument function assembling all these sections, the kernel, is symmetric and positive-definite, and the Moore-Aronszajn theorem guarantees that every such kernel corresponds to exactly one RKHS and vice versa, so the kernel and the space are two views of one object.

The reason this structure is so useful is that it makes the kernel trick rigorous. A positive-definite kernel can be written as an inner product of feature maps in the RKHS, so comparing two inputs with the kernel is exactly comparing their often infinite-dimensional feature representations by inner product, without ever forming those representations. This is what lets a kernel method operate in a vast or infinite feature space at the cost of evaluating a simple kernel on the original inputs: a linear method expressed entirely in inner products is lifted into a powerful nonlinear one simply by swapping the ordinary dot product for a kernel, with all the heavy lifting hidden inside the geometry of the space.

The bridge from this abstract space to a concrete finite computation is the representer theorem, and it is the linchpin. It states that the function minimizing a typical regularized learning objective, data-fit plus a penalty on the RKHS norm, always lies in the finite-dimensional subspace spanned by the kernel sections at the training points, no matter how infinite-dimensional the full space is. The optimal predictor is therefore a finite weighted sum of kernel evaluations against the training data, so an infinite-dimensional optimization collapses to solving for one coefficient per training example. This is precisely why the support vector machine, kernel ridge regression, and kernel PCA are tractable, reducing to convex optimization in finitely many variables, while the RKHS norm in the penalty controls smoothness and formalizes the bias toward simple functions that keeps them from overfitting.

The viewpoint reaches well beyond classical kernel machines. Gaussian processes are the probabilistic twin of kernel methods, with the covariance function playing the role of the kernel and the RKHS describing the functions the prior favors. The maximum mean embedding maps an entire probability distribution to a single point in an RKHS, giving the maximum mean discrepancy that powers two-sample tests and some generative objectives. The choice of kernel encodes prior structure: a translation-invariant kernel has, by Bochner's theorem, a spectral description via the Fourier transform, linking the smoothness of functions in the space to a frequency distribution, and recent work connects wide neural networks to a specific kernel, the neural tangent kernel, and so to a specific RKHS. Across all of these, the RKHS is the rigorous container that turns "compare things by a kernel" into a precise geometry of functions.
