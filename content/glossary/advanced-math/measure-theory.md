Measure theory answers a deceptively basic question: how do you assign a size to a set in a way that behaves sensibly? Length, area, volume, mass, and probability are all instances of the same idea, a rule that takes a set and returns a nonnegative number, adds up correctly over disjoint pieces, and assigns zero to nothing. The naive hope that every subset of the real line has a well-defined length turns out to be impossible to satisfy consistently, so the theory restricts attention to a carefully chosen collection of measurable sets, a sigma-algebra, closed under complements and countable unions. A measure is then a function on that collection that is countably additive: the measure of a countable disjoint union is the sum of the measures.

The motivating construction is the Lebesgue measure on the real line, which extends ordinary length to a vastly richer class of sets than intervals alone, and the Lebesgue integral built on top of it. Where the older Riemann integral slices the domain into thin vertical strips, the Lebesgue integral slices the range, asking how much of the domain maps into each thin horizontal band and weighting by the measure of that preimage. This reordering makes the integral robust: it handles wildly discontinuous functions, it interchanges cleanly with limits under mild conditions (the monotone and dominated convergence theorems), and it makes the space of integrable functions complete, which is exactly what later analysis needs.

The reason measure theory is indispensable to machine learning is that it is the rigorous language of probability. A probability is just a measure whose total size is one, and a probability-distribution is a measure on the space of outcomes. Once you adopt this view, the artificial split between discrete distributions (described by sums) and continuous ones (described by integrals) disappears: both are integrals against a measure, the only difference being whether the underlying measure is counting measure or Lebesgue measure. A random variable is a measurable function, its expectation is its integral against the probability measure, and conditional expectation is a projection defined precisely through measure-theoretic machinery.

The single most important tool the theory hands to practitioners is the Radon-Nikodym derivative, the density of one measure with respect to another. It is what lets you write a continuous density as the rate at which probability accumulates, and it is the object inside the logarithm in nearly every divergence used in the field. The kl-divergence, cross-entropy, and entropy are all expectations of log-densities, hence Radon-Nikodym derivatives under the hood. The change-of-variables formula that makes a normalizing-flow tractable is a measure-theoretic statement about how a measure transforms when you push it through an invertible map.

Measure theory also underpins the spaces where modern objects live and the limits that justify modern algorithms. Square-integrable functions form a Hilbert space only because the Lebesgue integral makes that space complete. The law of large numbers and the central limit theorem, which guarantee that averaging over data converges and that stochastic estimates behave, are theorems about convergence of measures. And optimal-transport, the geometry of moving one distribution onto another, is formulated entirely in terms of measures and measurable maps. You can train models without ever invoking these foundations explicitly, but every guarantee that the training is doing something well-defined rests on them.
