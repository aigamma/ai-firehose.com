Measure theory answers a deceptively basic question: how do you assign a size to a set in a way that behaves sensibly? Length, area, volume, mass, and probability are all the same idea, a rule that takes a set and returns a nonnegative number, adds correctly over disjoint pieces, and gives nothing to the empty set. The naive hope that every subset of the real line has a well-defined length turns out to be impossible to satisfy consistently, so the theory restricts attention to a carefully chosen collection of measurable sets, a sigma-algebra closed under complements and countable unions, and defines a measure as a function on that collection that is countably additive: the measure of a countable disjoint union is the sum of the measures.

The motivating construction is the Lebesgue measure, which extends ordinary length to a far richer class of sets than intervals, and the Lebesgue integral built on it. Where the older Riemann integral slices the domain into thin vertical strips, the Lebesgue integral slices the range, asking how much of the domain lands in each thin horizontal band and weighting by the measure of that preimage. This reordering makes the integral robust: it handles wildly discontinuous functions, interchanges cleanly with limits under mild conditions, and makes the space of integrable functions complete, which is exactly what later analysis needs to stand on.

Measure theory is indispensable to machine learning because it is the rigorous language of probability, and it quietly removes a distinction beginners trip over. A probability is just a measure whose total size is one, and once you adopt that view the artificial split between discrete distributions described by sums and continuous ones described by integrals disappears: both are integrals against a measure, differing only in whether the underlying measure counts points or measures length. A random variable becomes a measurable function, its expectation its integral against the probability measure, and conditional expectation a projection defined precisely through this machinery.

The single most important tool the theory hands a practitioner is the Radon-Nikodym derivative, the density of one measure with respect to another, and it is hiding inside nearly everything. It is what lets you write a density as the rate at which probability accumulates, and it is the object inside the logarithm in every divergence the field uses: KL divergence, cross-entropy, and entropy are all expectations of log-densities, Radon-Nikodym derivatives under the hood, and the change-of-variables formula that makes a normalizing flow tractable is a measure-theoretic statement about how a measure transforms under an invertible map. You can train models without ever naming these foundations, but every guarantee that the training means something well-defined rests on them.
