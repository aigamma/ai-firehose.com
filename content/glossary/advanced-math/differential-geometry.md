Differential geometry is calculus on curved spaces. Where ordinary calculus works on flat Euclidean space, differential geometry develops the same machinery, derivatives, integrals, vector fields, flows, on a smooth manifold, a space curved globally but flat in any small neighborhood. That local flatness is what makes calculus possible at all: at each point there is a tangent space, the flat linear approximation holding all the velocity vectors a curve through that point can have, and differentiation happens there. Stitching these local linear pictures together coherently across the whole manifold, while tracking how they twist relative to one another, is the technical core of the subject.

The structure that turns a bare smooth manifold into something you can measure is the metric tensor, a smoothly varying inner product on each tangent space; with it the manifold becomes a Riemannian manifold and acquires lengths, angles, and areas. From the metric you derive how to differentiate vector fields along the manifold, the connection and its covariant derivative, which defines the geodesic as a curve whose velocity is carried along without turning. The failure of this parallel transport to return a vector unchanged after a small loop is exactly curvature, the local quantity saying how far the space departs from flat. Metric, connection, and curvature are the working vocabulary of the field.

The subject's reach in mathematics and physics is enormous. Gauss's study of surfaces and Riemann's generalization to arbitrary dimensions culminated in the geometry Einstein used for general relativity, where gravity is curvature of spacetime, and the gauge theories describing the other fundamental forces are differential geometry as well, phrased as connections on fiber bundles. The same constructions feed back into pure mathematics through topology and through the theory of the Lie group, where a continuous symmetry group is itself a smooth manifold whose local structure is read with differential-geometric tools.

In machine learning the relevance is direct whenever a representation or parameter space is not flat. Treating a model's family of probability distributions as a curved manifold is information geometry, whose metric is the Fisher information, and natural gradient methods then step along the geometry that metric defines rather than in raw coordinates, so updates respect how much the model's outputs actually change. Embedding hierarchical data into negatively curved hyperbolic space fits trees with far less distortion than flat space allows, and optimizing over constrained sets like rotation or positive-definite matrices is Riemannian optimization along geodesics. The unifying lesson is that distance and direction are choices encoded by the metric, not fixed facts of the ambient coordinates: pick the metric and you have picked what nearness and straightness mean, and the curvature that results dictates how paths spread and how optimization behaves.
