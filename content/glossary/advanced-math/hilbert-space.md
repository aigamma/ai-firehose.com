A Hilbert space is what Euclidean geometry becomes when you take it to infinite dimensions without losing anything. It is a vector space carrying an inner product, the abstract version of the dot product, which supplies lengths through the norm it induces, angles between vectors, and a notion of orthogonality. The one extra requirement beyond having an inner product is completeness: every sequence whose terms eventually cluster arbitrarily close together must actually converge to a point in the space, with no gaps. Completeness is what lets you take limits, sum infinite series of vectors, and trust that constructions defined by approximation land somewhere, which is precisely what infinite-dimensional analysis needs.

The payoff of keeping the inner product is that geometric intuition survives intact. You can project a vector onto a subspace and the projection is the unique closest point, exactly as in the plane; you can expand any vector in an orthonormal basis with coefficients given by inner products and recover its squared length as the sum of squared coefficients. The canonical infinite-dimensional example is the space of square-integrable functions, which is a Hilbert space only because the Lebesgue integral of measure theory makes it complete, and the Fourier transform is then nothing but a change of orthonormal basis in that space, swapping localized impulses for periodic waves, two coordinate systems for the same function.

Hilbert spaces matter to machine learning most directly through kernel methods and the reproducing kernel Hilbert space. A positive-definite kernel implicitly maps inputs into a possibly infinite-dimensional feature Hilbert space and computes inner products there without ever forming the high-dimensional features, the kernel trick. This is what lets a support vector machine draw a linear separating boundary in a rich feature space, corresponding to a curved boundary in the original input, while doing all arithmetic through kernel evaluations. The reproducing property, that evaluating a function at a point equals an inner product with a special kernel element, is the technical hinge that makes this work and gives Gaussian processes and much of classical statistical learning their clean form.

The same geometry illuminates how modern representations behave. An embedding model maps text or images into a finite-dimensional inner-product space, a Hilbert space, and the cosine similarity used to compare embeddings is exactly the inner-product angle that space defines, so reasoning about projections, orthogonality, and nearest points in retrieval is reasoning in a Hilbert space. More broadly it is the natural arena for linear problems in infinite dimensions, underlying Fourier analysis, the spectral theory of operators, quantum mechanics, and signal processing. Its defining virtue is constant: whenever you have an inner product and can take limits safely, the full toolkit of Euclidean geometry, length, angle, projection, orthonormal expansion, continues to hold far beyond finite-dimensional intuition.
