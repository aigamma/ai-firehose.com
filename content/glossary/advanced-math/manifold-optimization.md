Many of the most natural objects in machine learning are not free vectors but constrained ones, and manifold optimization is how you optimize them honestly. A direction is a unit-norm vector and lives on a sphere; a rotation is an orthogonal matrix and lives on the orthogonal group; a subspace lives on a Grassmann manifold, a covariance matrix on the cone of positive-definite matrices, a low-rank matrix on a fixed-rank manifold. Treating these as ordinary unconstrained problems and bolting on penalties or repeatedly projecting back tends to be clumsy, slow, and numerically fragile. Manifold optimization, also called Riemannian optimization, instead builds the constraint into the search itself, so the iterate never leaves the valid set at any point.

The core idea is to replace each ingredient of ordinary gradient descent with its geometric counterpart on a Riemannian manifold. The ordinary gradient becomes the Riemannian gradient, the projection of the Euclidean gradient onto the tangent space at the current point, the steepest-ascent direction among the moves the manifold actually permits. A step is then taken not in a straight Euclidean line, which would leave the manifold, but along its surface, ideally following a geodesic via the exponential map, though in practice a cheaper retraction, any smooth map that moves along the tangent direction and lands back on the manifold to first order, is used because it is far less expensive and works just as well. Multi-step methods like momentum additionally need parallel transport to carry an accumulated direction from one tangent space to the next, since vectors at different points cannot simply be added.

This machinery matters because it converts a hard constrained problem into something that looks and behaves like familiar unconstrained optimization, just performed in the right geometry. Convergence theory carries over: Riemannian gradient methods enjoy the same kinds of guarantees as their flat counterparts, and a function that is convex along geodesics can be optimized with the strong guarantees of convex optimization even though it is not convex in the ambient coordinates. The geometry frequently reveals hidden structure too, exposing symmetries and removing spurious degrees of freedom that would otherwise stall an unconstrained solver.

In modern practice manifold optimization shows up wherever weights or representations carry a geometric constraint. Orthogonality constraints on recurrent and attention weight matrices, imposed on the orthogonal or Stiefel manifold, stabilize training by keeping the spectrum well-behaved and preventing gradients from vanishing or exploding. Training hyperbolic embeddings requires Riemannian updates so points stay inside the model of hyperbolic space and distances stay correct, estimating covariance respects the positive-definite cone, and principal component analysis and subspace tracking are naturally Grassmann or Stiefel problems. Mature libraries make these steps turnkey, so a practitioner names the manifold and the cost and the optimizer handles the projection, retraction, and transport. It is, in the end, the operational payoff of differential geometry for machine learning: once you accept that your variables live on a curved set, this is how you do descent there correctly.
