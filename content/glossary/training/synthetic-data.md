Synthetic data is training data produced by a model instead of collected from the world. It has moved from a stopgap to a central pillar of how modern language models are built, because the supply of high-quality human text and labels is finite while the appetite of training is not, and when the data a model needs does not exist, is too expensive to gather, or is sensitive to handle, a capable model can often manufacture it.

The uses are broad. Instruction tuning is frequently driven by model-generated prompt and response pairs; preference data for alignment can come from a model judging its own outputs, the idea behind reinforcement learning from AI feedback and related to constitutional AI; reasoning models are trained on large volumes of model-generated reasoning traces filtered to keep only those reaching a verified correct answer; and a smaller model can be taught by the outputs of a stronger one, the data side of knowledge distillation.

The decisive issue is quality control, and it bites hard, because a model trained on a model's mistakes inherits and can amplify them. Training a model repeatedly on its own unfiltered output risks model collapse, a gradual narrowing where the diversity of the original data distribution is lost and rare cases vanish, and biases and errors propagate the same way. The practices that make synthetic data work are therefore filtering and verification: keeping only traces that pass a checker, grounding generation in real sources, having a stronger model or a tool validate outputs, and blending synthetic data with genuine human data rather than replacing it.

Used with that discipline, synthetic data lets teams aim capability where they want it, generating targeted examples of skills a model is weak at, and it scales in a way human annotation cannot. The art is in the verification, not the generation.
