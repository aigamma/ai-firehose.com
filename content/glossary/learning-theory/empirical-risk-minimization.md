Empirical risk minimization, ERM, names what almost all supervised learning actually does. The quantity you care about is the true risk, the expected loss over the entire data distribution, but that distribution is unknown and the expectation cannot be computed. ERM substitutes the empirical risk, the average loss over the finite training sample you do have, and picks the model that minimizes this observable surrogate. Fitting a model to its training data by minimizing a loss is ERM in action, whatever the loss, squared error, cross-entropy, or hinge.

The justification is the law of large numbers: as the sample grows, the empirical risk converges to the true risk, so the minimizer of the one should approach the minimizer of the other. The subtlety, and it is the whole game, is that this convergence must hold uniformly across the entire hypothesis class, not just for a single fixed model, because ERM searches over many candidates and will happily seize on any one that looks good on the sample by luck. Guaranteeing uniform convergence is precisely the job of statistical learning theory, which is what ties ERM to generalization and to the PAC framework, whose guarantees are exactly the conditions under which minimizing empirical risk drives true risk down.

So whether ERM succeeds depends entirely on the capacity of the hypothesis class. If the class is too rich, the model fits the sample perfectly, noise included, reaching near-zero empirical risk while the true risk stays high, which is overfitting, and the size of that gap is controlled by capacity measures like the VC dimension. The fundamental theorem of statistical learning says ERM over a class of finite VC dimension generalizes, with the data requirement set by the sample complexity, so plain ERM is safe only when the class carries enough inductive bias to keep its effective capacity in check. When unconstrained ERM would overfit, it is tamed by adding structure: regularized risk minimization adds a penalty on complexity, an explicit nod to Occam's razor, and structural risk minimization organizes hypotheses into nested classes of growing capacity and picks the level that best balances fit against complexity.

ERM also frames one of the open puzzles of deep learning. Modern networks driven to zero empirical risk, even on randomly labeled data, should by classical reasoning overfit badly, yet they frequently generalize well, and the double descent curve shows test error improving as such interpolating models grow. The reconciliation is that ERM by gradient descent does not pick an arbitrary zero-loss solution but is implicitly biased toward particular well-behaved ones, and understanding this implicit regularization, why the optimizer's chosen minimizer of the empirical risk also minimizes the true risk in the overparameterized regime, is among the deepest open questions in the field.
