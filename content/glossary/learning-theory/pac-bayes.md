PAC-Bayes bounds generalization not for a single hypothesis but for a distribution over hypotheses. Instead of picking one model, the learner is thought of as choosing a posterior distribution Q over the whole hypothesis class, starting from a prior P fixed before any data is seen. A PAC-Bayes theorem then guarantees, with high probability, that the expected true error of a hypothesis drawn from Q exceeds its expected training error by no more than a term growing with the KL divergence between Q and P, divided by the sample size. The framework marries the worst-case rigor of PAC learning with the distributional language of Bayesian inference, which is the source of its name.

That KL-divergence penalty is a remarkably tight and flexible measure of complexity. A posterior that barely moves from the prior pays almost nothing and is guaranteed to generalize; a posterior that contorts itself far from the prior to fit the data pays a large penalty and is warned it may not transfer. This is a precise, quantitative statement of Occam's razor: the bound rewards explanations that required little information beyond what the prior already encoded. Unlike a bound based on the VC dimension, which depends only on the hypothesis class, the PAC-Bayes bound depends on the specific solution the learner found and on the prior it started from, so it can be tight for the model actually in hand.

That tightness is why PAC-Bayes produces some of the only non-vacuous generalization bounds for deep networks, a regime where classical theory fails completely. VC and parameter-counting bounds for a network with billions of weights are astronomically larger than one and so say nothing, but by choosing the posterior cleverly, often a distribution over weights centered on the trained network with a noise scale the network can tolerate, researchers have computed PAC-Bayes bounds genuinely below one and thus meaningful. The key insight connects the bound to optimization: a network whose predictions are robust to weight perturbation corresponds to a posterior with a small KL term, so flatness of the found minimum translates directly into a better bound.

The framework also makes explicit why the priors and biases of a learning procedure are decisive rather than incidental. The prior P is a free choice, and one aligned with the structure of real problems yields a small KL divergence and a strong guarantee, the formal echo of the lesson that all generalization rests on an inductive bias matched to the world. PAC-Bayes makes that bias chargeable: the cost of learning is literally the information distance traveled from prior belief to posterior, and a well-chosen prior makes good solutions cheap to reach. Standing as a third pillar of statistical learning theory beside the VC and Rademacher approaches, its enduring message is that generalization is governed less by the size of the hypothesis class than by how little the data forced the learner to depart from what it already believed.
