A concentration inequality states that a random quantity built from many independent pieces stays close to its mean with high probability. The simplest concerns an average: average a large number of independent measurements, each bounded in some range, and the result is overwhelmingly likely to land within a small distance of its true expected value, with the probability of a large deviation falling off exponentially in the number of measurements. This phenomenon, that aggregates of many independent randomnesses are nearly deterministic, is called concentration of measure, and the inequalities quantifying it are the workhorses of probabilistic analysis in machine learning.

They matter to learning theory because its central question is a deviation question in disguise. A model's training error is an average of its losses over a finite random sample, and its true error is the expectation of that loss over the whole distribution, so asking whether training error reflects true error is exactly asking whether this particular average has concentrated near its mean. A concentration inequality answers it: with the sample size plugged in, it bounds the probability that the two differ by more than a chosen amount. This is why every generalization bound, at its core, is a concentration inequality applied to the right random variable.

Hoeffding's inequality is the canonical instance: the average of independent random variables, each confined to a bounded interval, deviates from its mean by more than epsilon with probability at most an exponentially small quantity depending on epsilon squared and the sample size. Rearranged, it tells you how many samples make the deviation small with high confidence, which is precisely the sample complexity for a single fixed hypothesis. Markov's and Chebyshev's inequalities are cruder ancestors; Bernstein's and McDiarmid's are sharper or more general successors, extending the idea to variance-aware bounds and to functions more complicated than a plain average.

The subtlety that makes concentration the heart of learning theory, rather than a one-line application, is the move from one hypothesis to many. Hoeffding controls the deviation for a single predictor chosen in advance, but empirical risk minimization searches an entire class and deliberately picks the one that looks best on the sample, which can exploit a lucky deviation anywhere in the class. The fix is a uniform concentration bound holding simultaneously over all hypotheses, paid for by a complexity term, a union bound for finite classes, the VC dimension or Rademacher complexity for infinite ones, that accounts for how many effective ways the class had to overfit. The whole apparatus of capacity exists to upgrade single-hypothesis concentration into uniform concentration, which is the reason finite samples can certify anything about an infinite distribution at all, and why more data helps but with diminishing, one-over-root-n returns.
