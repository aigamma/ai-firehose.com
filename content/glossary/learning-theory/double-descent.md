Double descent is the empirical curve that broke the textbook picture of overfitting. Classical theory predicts a U-shaped relationship between capacity and test error: error falls as the model gains enough flexibility to capture the signal, then rises as added flexibility lets it chase noise. Double descent shows that U is only the first half of the story. As capacity keeps growing past the point where the model exactly interpolates the training data, test error, after spiking, begins to fall again, often reaching a new minimum below the bottom of the classical U. The peak in the middle is the interpolation threshold, where the model has just barely enough degrees of freedom to drive training error to zero.

The shape comes in several flavors: model-wise double descent varies the number of parameters, sample-wise varies the dataset size, and epoch-wise varies training time, the characteristic peak appearing as a function of each. The danger zone is always the interpolation threshold, where the effective number of parameters roughly equals the number of training points; there the model is forced to fit every example with no slack, so it latches onto noise and produces brittle, high-variance solutions. Push past that boundary into heavy overparameterization and the behavior reverses, error descending a second time. The term was popularized by Belkin and collaborators around 2019 and demonstrated at scale in deep networks by Nakkiran and colleagues.

Double descent matters because it reconciles two facts that once seemed contradictory: classical theory warns against more parameters than data, yet the largest and most overparameterized networks generalize best. The resolution is that capacity counted crudely, by the VC dimension or raw parameter count, is the wrong axis once a model can interpolate. Beyond the threshold many distinct zero-training-error solutions exist, and the relevant question is which one the learning procedure selects: the optimizer's inductive bias, its tendency toward low-norm, smooth, or flat solutions, picks a well-behaved interpolant out of the many available, and that implicit preference is what makes the second descent possible.

The phenomenon reshapes practical intuition about generalization and model selection. The old fear that more parameters than samples guarantees disaster is wrong in the overparameterized regime, and the historically risky choice is in fact the middle, near interpolation, where one should either back off or push well past. It also gives a subtler reading of Occam's razor: simplicity is not the same as fewer parameters, since a huge network can encode a simple function through the bias of how it is trained. The honest lesson is that what controls generalization is not raw capacity but the effective complexity of the solution the training process actually lands on.
