Bayesian inference is the approach to statistics that represents everything unknown, including a model's parameters, as a probability distribution rather than a single fixed value. It begins with a prior distribution encoding belief about the unknowns before seeing data, combines it with the likelihood of the observed data, and produces a posterior distribution representing updated belief afterward, the update governed by Bayes' theorem: the posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior. The defining move is that inference returns a whole distribution over possibilities, not a lone point estimate, so uncertainty is carried explicitly through every conclusion rather than discarded at the first step.

This framework matters because it answers questions point estimation cannot. A maximum-likelihood fit gives one best parameter setting but says nothing about how confident that setting is, whereas the Bayesian posterior directly reports the variance and the credible intervals of each unknown. Predictions are made by averaging over the entire posterior, the posterior predictive distribution, which naturally accounts for parameter uncertainty and tends to be better calibrated than predictions from a single fitted model. With little data the posterior stays appropriately wide; as evidence accumulates it concentrates and the prior's influence fades. Knowing how much you do not know is the whole value proposition.

The relationship to maximum-likelihood estimation is precise and worth holding onto, because it shows the familiar methods are special cases. Taking the single most probable point of the posterior gives maximum a posteriori estimation, which equals the likelihood maximizer regularized by the prior; when the prior is flat, it collapses back to plain maximum likelihood. So the familiar point estimators are impoverished Bayesian procedures, recovered by throwing away everything about the posterior except its peak. Many common regularizers are exactly log-priors in disguise: an L2 penalty is a Gaussian prior on the weights, an L1 penalty a Laplace prior, so regularization is Bayesian inference smuggled in under another name.

The central practical obstacle is that the posterior is usually intractable, because the normalizing constant in Bayes' theorem, the marginal likelihood, requires integrating over all possible parameter values, and that integral rarely has a closed form. This is why Bayesian inference in practice is largely the study of approximation. Markov chain Monte Carlo methods draw samples from the posterior without computing the normalizer; variational inference replaces the true posterior with a simpler distribution and fits it by minimizing the KL divergence between them, turning integration into optimization. Bayesian thinking runs through modern machine learning even where it is unnamed, in variational autoencoders, Bayesian neural networks, Gaussian processes, and the Bayesian optimization behind much hyperparameter tuning. Wherever a system must reason under uncertainty and report how much it does not know, this is the principled framework for doing so.
