An energy-based model, or EBM, models data through a single learned scalar function called the energy. The energy takes a configuration, an image, a sentence, or a pairing of an input with a candidate output, and returns a number that is low when the configuration is plausible or compatible and high when it is not. Learning means shaping this energy landscape so real data sits in the valleys and everything else sits on the hills, and unlike a standard classifier or regressor that maps an input directly to an answer, an EBM only judges how good a complete configuration is, so answering a question becomes a search for the lowest-energy configuration consistent with what is given.

This indirection is the source of the framework's appeal. An energy function defines a probability distribution by the rule that probability falls off exponentially as energy rises, normalized by a quantity called the partition function, and because the model is just an unconstrained scalar function, it can capture rich dependencies and represent distributions with many simultaneous valleys, awkward for a model forced to output a single prediction. The same energy can also be queried in many directions: given part of a configuration it can score or fill in the rest, which makes EBMs natural for structured prediction, denoising, and reasoning about compatibility rather than committing to one feed-forward output.

The central difficulty is the keeper: the partition function, the sum or integral of the exponentiated energy over every possible configuration, is generally intractable to compute. As a result EBMs are usually trained without ever evaluating it. Contrastive methods push the energy down on observed data and pull it up on contrasting negative samples, so the landscape is sculpted by comparison rather than by exact likelihood, and drawing those negative samples, and sampling from a trained EBM, typically relies on gradient-based procedures that descend the energy with injected noise, a process that ties EBMs closely to score-based and diffusion generative models, which can be read as learning the gradient of an energy.

Energy-based models are a long-running and unifying idea rather than a single architecture, and they connect broadly across machine learning. Classical Hopfield networks and Boltzmann machines were early EBMs, and the modern revival positions the energy function as a general substrate: it underlies one prominent research agenda for world models, where a learned energy scores how compatible a predicted future is with the present instead of generating it pixel by pixel. The framework also pairs naturally with the continuous dynamics of the neural ODE, since descending an energy is itself the flow of a differential equation, and it offers a probabilistic lens that complements the sequence-mixing focus of the other frontier architectures in this category.
