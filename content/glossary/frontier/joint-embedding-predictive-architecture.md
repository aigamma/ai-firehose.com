A joint embedding predictive architecture, or JEPA, learns representations by prediction in a feature space rather than in the space of raw inputs. It encodes a visible context and a hidden target part of the same signal into embeddings, then trains a predictor to map the context embedding to the target embedding, and the defining choice is what is predicted: not the missing pixels or tokens themselves but their learned representation. Promoted by Yann LeCun as a centerpiece of his program for machine intelligence and realized in image and video systems from 2023 onward, it is offered as a path toward models that build usable internal structure from observation alone.

The motivation is a critique of the two dominant self-supervised recipes. Generative reconstruction, as in a masked autoencoder, forces the model to predict every detail of the missing input, including texture and noise that are inherently unpredictable, so it wastes capacity modeling irrelevant specifics; contrastive methods avoid reconstruction but need carefully chosen negative examples to work. A JEPA aims to keep the simplicity of prediction while ducking both problems: by predicting in an abstract embedding space, it can represent that a region contains, say, a dog's head without committing to the exact arrangement of fur, discarding the unpredictable detail and keeping the semantic content, which is meant to yield representations organized around meaning rather than appearance.

Predicting in representation space introduces a hazard that shapes the whole design, and naming it is the keeper: representation collapse. If both the target encoder and the predictor are free to move, the easiest way to make predictions accurate is to make every embedding identical, a constant solution that is trivially predictable and entirely useless. JEPAs prevent this with architectural asymmetry rather than negatives, typically by computing the target embeddings with an encoder whose weights are an exponentially moving average of the context encoder and are not updated by the gradient, so the target cannot freely collapse toward the prediction. The arrangement can be read in the language of an energy-based model, where training shapes a landscape that assigns low energy to compatible context-target pairs and high energy elsewhere.

The architecture matters as a concrete proposal for learning world-model-like structure from unlabeled data. The image version predicts the embeddings of masked image regions from a visible context, and the video version predicts masked spatiotemporal regions, both using a vision transformer as the encoder; the video form in particular is pitched as a step toward models that internalize how scenes evolve and how objects move, the kind of predictive understanding a world model needs. Whether prediction in embedding space is the right route to broad machine understanding remains an open and debated question, but JEPA is one of the most clearly articulated bets that it is, a deliberate alternative to the generative and contrastive paradigms that have dominated self-supervised learning.
