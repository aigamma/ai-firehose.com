RWKV, short for Receptance Weighted Key Value, is designed to have it both ways: train like a transformer and run at inference like a recurrent network. Its central claim is a dual formulation, the same model admitting a parallel form where all positions are computed at once for efficient training, and an equivalent recurrent form where generation proceeds one token at a time with a fixed-size state. Introduced by Peng and a large open collaboration in 2023, it was one of the first architectures to demonstrate transformer-class language-modeling quality while keeping the cheap, stateful inference of an rnn at scale.

The architecture attacks the transformer's core inference cost head-on. Generating with attention requires keeping a kv-cache that grows linearly with the sequence, so memory use and per-token cost climb without bound as context lengthens; an old-style recurrent neural network has the opposite profile, constant memory and linear time, but is hard to train in parallel and struggles with long-range dependencies. RWKV captures the favorable side of each: the recurrent form carries a fixed-size state, so it generates in constant memory regardless of how much text has been produced, while the parallel form sidesteps the slow sequential training that historically held recurrent models back.

Mechanically RWKV replaces self-attention with two interacting blocks built from learned, channel-wise decay. A time-mixing block plays the role of attention by mixing information across positions, but instead of computing all-pairs interactions it maintains a running, exponentially weighted sum of past key and value signals, gated by a receptance term that decides how much of that accumulated context to admit; a channel-mixing block plays the role of the feedforward layer. The exponential time-decay is what lets the running state be updated recurrently with a single fixed-size carry, the algebraic trick that makes the parallel and recurrent forms equivalent, placing RWKV firmly in the family of linear attention and the retentive network, all of which obtain a recurrent form by giving up the unrestricted all-pairs interaction of softmax attention.

RWKV is significant as a sustained, openly developed line of attention-free models, advanced across successive versions and trained at sizes competitive with mainstream open transformers, which is the keeper: it proved the approach through a community effort rather than a single lab. Later versions refined the decay and mixing to close quality gaps and handle long context better, addressing the usual weakness of fixed-state models, that a single bounded state must summarize an unbounded past and can lose precise long-range recall that explicit attention retains. It stands alongside mamba and the broader state space model family as a leading attempt to find an efficient successor to the transformer.
