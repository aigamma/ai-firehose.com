There is a persistent gap between what AI can do and what is actually being done with it. Capability jumps in a single model release; adoption crawls, because using a new capability means changing how people work, and people and organizations change slowly. AI adoption is the study of that lag: not what is possible, but what has crossed the distance into real, durable use.

The barriers are mostly human and organizational. A capable tool still has to be trusted with real stakes, integrated into existing systems, and learned by the people who will use it, and each of those takes longer than training the model did. There is also a measurement problem: early adoption often shows up as experimentation that does not yet move any business number, which makes it easy to overstate or understate how much has really changed.

Capability and adoption are different curves moving at different speeds, and confusing them is the most common error in reading the field. The technology can be improving sharply while its economic impact arrives gradually, exactly as electricity and computing did, where the productivity gains showed up decades after the invention because the world had to reorganize around them. The variable that binds is organizational absorption, not raw capability.

That reframes the interesting question. It is less "what can the model do" and more "what is stopping the capability we already have from being used", because that gap, not the frontier, is where most of the near-term value and disruption actually live. Adoption, not invention, is usually the slower and more decisive variable.
