The technical story of AI, bigger models and better benchmarks, sits on top of an economic one that is just as consequential and far less settled: who pays for the compute, who captures the value, and whether the returns will ever justify the spending. Market dynamics is the lens that treats AI as an industry, asking not what a model can do but what its existence does to prices, profits, moats, and capital.

A few forces dominate the conversation. Training frontier models costs enormous sums up front, which concentrates capability in a handful of well-funded labs and raises the question of whether anyone recoups it. Inference, the cost of actually running models for users, is the recurring bill that decides whether a product is profitable. And the rapid commoditization of capability, where last year's frontier becomes this year's cheap open model, keeps compressing margins and forcing the question of where a durable advantage can live when the core technology refuses to stay scarce.

The central tension is between the cost curve and the value curve. Costs are concrete, measurable, and being spent now, at the scale of national infrastructure; the value is projected, diffuse, and largely still ahead. That gap is why the same numbers support both the bubble thesis, capital racing ahead of revenue with no clear path to returns, and the buildout thesis, that this is the early, unprofitable phase of infrastructure that pays off for decades, as railways and fiber once did. Which frame is right is unknown, and the honest analyst holds both.

What market dynamics reveals is that the winners of the AI era will not be decided by model quality alone. When capability commoditizes, the advantage shifts to whatever stays scarce: distribution, proprietary data, integration into workflows people cannot leave, and the trust to be relied on. The interesting question is therefore less which lab has the best model this quarter and more where, in a stack whose bottom layer keeps getting cheaper, the value finally settles.
