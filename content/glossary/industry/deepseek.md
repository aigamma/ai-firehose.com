DeepSeek is a Chinese AI lab that drew outsized attention not for being the largest or most capable but for being startlingly efficient: it released strong, open-weight models reportedly trained at a small fraction of the cost the leading labs were spending. That combination, frontier-adjacent capability, open weights, and a low training bill, made it a reference point in nearly every argument about where AI is heading.

Its impact was about expectations more than any single model. The prevailing story had been that frontier capability required enormous, concentrated compute budgets only a few firms could afford, which justified closed models and high prices. DeepSeek's efficient, open releases complicated that story, suggesting that clever engineering could narrow the gap cheaply and openly, which rattled assumptions about moats, valuations, and the inevitability of a closed, capital-gated frontier.

DeepSeek matters most as evidence in the field's central economic debate: whether the advantage in AI is durable scale or fleeting efficiency. If frontier-adjacent results can be reached far more cheaply and then given away, the moat that justifies the spending and the secrecy looks thinner, and power tilts toward whoever innovates on efficiency rather than whoever spends the most. Whether that reading holds is unsettled, but DeepSeek is the example people reach for when making the case.
