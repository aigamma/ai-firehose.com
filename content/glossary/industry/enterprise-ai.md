A frontier model that dazzles in a demo can stall completely inside a large company, and the reason is rarely the model. Enterprise AI is the work of closing the distance between "this works on a slide" and "this runs in our business", and that distance is filled almost entirely with non-model problems: data trapped in incompatible systems, rules about what may be sent where, approval chains, and the question of who is accountable when a probabilistic system is wrong.

The recurring blockers are concrete. The valuable data is proprietary, messy, and access-controlled, so feeding it to a model is an integration and governance project before it is an AI one. The output has to be trustworthy enough to act on, which pushes toward retrieval grounding, citations, guardrails, and a human in the loop on consequential steps. And the system must fit existing workflows and audits, because a tool people cannot trust or explain goes unused no matter how capable it is.

The thing to keep is that the frontier model is the commodity layer, available to every competitor on equal terms, so it is never the source of advantage. What is scarce, and therefore where the value settles, is the proprietary data wired in cleanly, the integration into workflows people cannot easily leave, and the institutional trust to rely on the system. Enterprise AI rewards the unglamorous plumbing, not the choice of model.

This is why enterprise adoption lags the headlines: capability arrives in a quarter, but the data integration, security review, and change management take far longer, and they are the actual project. The firms that win are usually not the ones with a better model, since everyone has that, but the ones that did the boring integration and earned the trust to put the model where the work happens.
