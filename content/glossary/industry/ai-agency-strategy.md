A genre of business advice has formed around a simple pitch: use AI to do work clients used to pay people for, and keep the margin. The AI agency, building automations, agents, websites, or content for businesses, is the most promoted version, sold in courses and videos as a fast path to a high-margin company. The opportunity is real, because AI genuinely lets a small team deliver what once took many, but the framing deserves the skepticism any get-rich playbook earns.

Underneath the hype, the actual strategy question is where a defensible advantage comes from, because the AI is not it. Everyone has the same models, so a business built only on access to them has no moat and competes on price toward zero. The durable advantages are the ordinary ones: a specific niche understood deeply, relationships and trust with clients, a reputation for reliable delivery, and the unglamorous integration work of wiring AI into a particular industry's real workflows. The AI is the commodity input; the business is everything around it.

The tell that separates the substance from the sales pitch is what is being sold. The genuine operators talk about a niche, a client, and a delivered result; the hype talks about the tool and the income screenshot. Much of the AI-agency content is itself the business, selling the dream of the business to people who will buy a course rather than land a client, which is the oldest pattern in get-rich advice wearing a new technology. The opportunity is real, and the median outcome is not the one in the thumbnail.

What the AI-agency moment actually reveals is a broader truth about this era: when a powerful capability becomes cheap and universal, value migrates away from the capability and toward distribution, trust, and domain depth, the things that stay scarce. The strategy that works is therefore not an AI strategy at all but an ordinary business one, applied to a moment when AI has lowered the cost of delivery, and the people who understand that the model is the least defensible part are the ones likely to last.
