The feed-forward network, almost always abbreviated FFN, is the second of the two sublayers that make up a transformer block. Attention gets most of the attention, but every transformer layer pairs a self-attention sublayer with an FFN, and the two play complementary roles: attention moves information between positions, letting tokens gather context from one another, while the FFN then processes each position on its own, transforming the gathered representation without any further mixing across the sequence. It is the part of the layer where a token thinks about what it has just collected.

Mechanically the FFN is a simple two-layer network applied independently at every position, which is why it is also called the position-wise feed-forward network. The first linear layer projects the token's vector up to a larger inner dimension, typically four times the model dimension, an activation function introduces a nonlinearity, and a second linear layer projects back down to the model dimension. The same two weight matrices are shared across all positions, so the FFN is small in parameter count per layer relative to its effect, yet because the inner dimension is wide it holds a large fraction of a transformer's total parameters. Modern models often swap the classic ReLU for smoother gated variants like GeLU or SwiGLU, which tend to train slightly better.

Although it is the architecturally plain half of the block, the FFN does heavy conceptual work, and the keeper is how to read it. A useful lens is a key-value memory: the up-projection compares the input against a large bank of learned patterns, the activation selects which of them fire, and the down-projection writes a combination of stored values back into the residual stream. Much interpretability research locates a model's factual and lexical knowledge in these layers, treating the FFN as where retrieved facts and learned associations live, while attention handles routing and composition. This division, mixing in attention and per-token computation in the FFN, is the basic rhythm repeated layer after layer.

The FFN connects to the rest of the architecture through the surrounding plumbing. Its output is added back to the input by a residual connection and stabilized by layer normalization, the same wrapping that surrounds the attention sublayer, so gradients flow cleanly through a deep stack. Because the FFN accounts for so much of the parameter budget and so much of the compute at inference, it is also the natural target for efficiency work: a mixture-of-experts layer replaces a single dense FFN with many smaller expert FFNs and routes each token to only a few, buying capacity without paying the full cost on every token. Understanding the FFN is therefore essential to understanding both where a transformer keeps its knowledge and where its compute is spent.
