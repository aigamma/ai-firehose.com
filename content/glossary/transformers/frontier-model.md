What counts as a frontier model is a deliberately moving target. The term names the few most capable general-purpose models at any given moment, the ones pushing the boundary of what AI can do, but today's frontier models are surpassed within months, so it marks a position at the leading edge rather than any fixed system. In practice it points to the largest, newest, most expensive-to-train models from the small set of labs that can build them, the ones topping benchmarks and human-preference rankings.

The phrase carries weight beyond marketing because of safety and policy, which is what matters. "Frontier AI" has become the label for models whose novel and not-yet-understood capabilities, and the risks that come with them, warrant special scrutiny, from voluntary safety commitments to proposed regulation. Calling a model a frontier model is partly a claim about capability and partly a claim that it sits in the zone where new risks first appear.

What distinguishes a frontier model is the combination the field's trends predict: the scale that scaling laws reward, the emergent abilities that appear only at that scale, and increasingly the inference-time reasoning that the newest systems add. It is less a fixed threshold than the running maximum of several curves at once.

That is the deep oddity of the term: it names not a technology but a boundary, and the boundary is defined by what no one has yet mapped. A frontier model is always the one whose capabilities are least understood, because the moment they are fully charted the frontier has already moved past them, which is exactly why the frontier is where both the next breakthrough and the next unanticipated risk tend to appear first.
