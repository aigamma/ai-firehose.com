Oversmoothing is the central reason most graph neural networks are shallow. Each layer of a graph neural network performs message passing, mixing every node's representation with those of its neighbors, and a single round is a smoothing operation: neighboring nodes are pulled toward one another. That is desirable in moderation, since it is how a node absorbs context from its surroundings, but repeated indefinitely, smoothing does not stop at a useful blend; it converges. After many layers, the representations of all nodes in a connected component drift toward a single common vector, the differences shrinking geometrically with depth until the features are nearly indistinguishable, and a classifier sitting on top of such collapsed representations cannot tell the nodes apart, so accuracy degrades sharply as layers are added. This is why naively deepening a GNN, the move that reliably helps convolutional and transformer networks, tends to hurt.

The phenomenon has a clean explanation through spectral graph theory, and it is the keeper. Neighborhood aggregation by the normalized graph operator is closely related to a diffusion or random walk on the graph, and repeatedly applying it acts as a low-pass filter: it preserves the lowest-frequency component of the signal on the graph and suppresses everything else. As depth grows the filter becomes ever more aggressively low-pass, and in the limit only the smoothest signal survives, which on a connected graph is essentially constant across nodes. Oversmoothing is therefore not a quirk of a particular implementation but a structural consequence of stacking smoothing operators, and the rate of collapse is governed by the spectral gap of the graph, itself a measure of how well-connected the graph is.

Oversmoothing is distinct from, though often confused with, two neighboring difficulties. Ordinary overfitting and vanishing gradients are generic deep-network problems; oversmoothing is specific to the graph mixing operation and would occur even with perfect optimization. Over-squashing is its complement: where oversmoothing is information becoming too uniform across the graph, over-squashing is information from an exponentially growing receptive field being crushed through a fixed-width representation across a graph bottleneck, so distant signals fail to arrive at all. Together these two effects squeeze the useful depth of a GNN from both ends, one punishing too much mixing and the other punishing too little reach.

A large body of techniques exists to push the depth ceiling higher. Residual and skip connections (a residual connection lets a node retain its own earlier representation), initial-residual schemes that re-inject the input features at every layer, and dense connectivity all give nodes a way to resist being averaged into uniformity; normalization methods designed for graphs, such as PairNorm, explicitly keep node representations spread apart by controlling pairwise distances or total variance. Architectural choices help too: decoupling the propagation steps from the feature transformations, attention-based aggregation that learns to down-weight irrelevant neighbors, jumping-knowledge connections that let the readout select information from the most informative depth, and graph rewiring that adds shortcut edges to relieve bottlenecks. Understanding oversmoothing as a low-pass effect is what makes these remedies legible: each one is a way to retain the high-frequency, node-specific signal that plain message passing would otherwise erase.
