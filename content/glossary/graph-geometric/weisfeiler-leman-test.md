The Weisfeiler-Leman test is a fast heuristic for the graph isomorphism problem: deciding whether two graphs are the same up to a relabeling of their nodes. Introduced by Boris Weisfeiler and Andrei Leman in 1968, its one-dimensional form works by color refinement: every node starts with the same color, and in each round a node combines its own color with the multiset of its neighbors' colors and hashes the result into a new color, so two nodes keep the same color only if their neighborhoods looked the same at the previous round. The colors are refined until the partition of nodes into color classes stops changing, and to compare two graphs you run the procedure on both and check whether the final histograms of colors match. If the histograms differ, the graphs are provably not isomorphic; if they match, the graphs might be isomorphic but the test cannot be sure.

The test matters far beyond classical algorithms because it is the exact yardstick for the expressive power of a graph neural network. The computation a standard GNN performs, message passing, is structurally the same as one round of color refinement: each node aggregates information from its neighbors and updates its own state. A landmark result, established around 2019 by Xu and colleagues and independently by Morris and colleagues, makes this precise: a message-passing GNN can distinguish two graphs only if the one-dimensional Weisfeiler-Leman test can distinguish them, and with sufficiently expressive aggregation and update functions it matches that bound exactly. The test therefore sets a hard ceiling on what neighborhood-aggregating architectures can tell apart, regardless of depth, width, or training.

That ceiling has concrete and sometimes surprising consequences. The one-dimensional test, and so ordinary message passing, cannot distinguish certain structurally different graphs that produce identical local neighborhood patterns: many regular graphs, and in particular it cannot count substructures like triangles or larger cycles reliably. Two molecules with genuinely different ring systems can collapse to the same representation, a real limitation for chemistry and for any task where global structure or motif counts matter. Recognizing this limit reframed a great deal of graph learning research as a search for architectures that climb above the Weisfeiler-Leman bound, and the Graph Isomorphism Network was designed specifically to reach the one-dimensional limit, using a sum aggregator and an injective update so no information is lost, which clarified why summation is more expressive than mean or max pooling in message passing.

The Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy also charts the way forward. Higher-dimensional variants, the k-dimensional Weisfeiler-Leman algorithms, color tuples of k nodes rather than single nodes and form a strictly increasing ladder of distinguishing power, and corresponding higher-order GNNs match these stronger tests at the cost of much greater computation. Other routes to more expressive models add features the one-dimensional test lacks: unique or random node identifiers, positional and structural encodings, explicit subgraph counts, or attention over larger neighborhoods. Across all of this, the Weisfeiler-Leman test is the common reference frame in geometric deep learning for stating precisely how powerful a graph model is, why one architecture separates graphs another cannot, and what it would take to do better.
