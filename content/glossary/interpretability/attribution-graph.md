An attribution graph is a map of how a model arrived at one particular answer, drawn in terms of interpretable features rather than raw neurons. The nodes are features active on the input, typically the monosemantic features recovered by a sparse-autoencoder, and the edges are the causal influences between them: which active features pushed which later features on, and how those in turn pushed the output token. Read end to end, the graph shows the chain of intermediate computations the model ran for this prompt, the steps it took on the way from the words it read to the word it produced. Where a circuit is the reusable mechanism a model uses across many inputs, an attribution graph is the trace of that mechanism firing on a single concrete example, the keeper, which makes it a way to see a model's reasoning in action.

The method matters because it scales the circuit-finding ambition of mechanistic interpretability from toy behaviors toward the full computation of a frontier model. Manually localizing a circuit with activation patching is powerful but slow and narrow; attribution graphs aim to surface the relevant features and their interactions more automatically, so that the multi-step structure of a model's behavior can be inspected at a glance. This is what lets researchers ask whether a model genuinely plans ahead, whether it computes an answer through a faithful chain of steps or shortcuts through a memorized association, and whether the reasoning it states in its output matches the reasoning its internals actually performed, which is directly relevant to detecting unfaithful or deceptive behavior.

Mechanically, attribution graphs are built on top of a feature decomposition and a way to estimate causal strength between features cheaply. Because directly patching every pair of features would be prohibitively expensive, the influences are often estimated with attribution patching, a linear approximation that uses gradients to predict the effect of an intervention without re-running the model for each one, trading some exactness for the ability to score enormous numbers of feature interactions at once. A common practical ingredient is a replacement layer of features, sometimes a cross-layer variant, that stands in for the model's own components while remaining interpretable, so that the graph is drawn over units a person can name. The estimated edges are then pruned to the ones that actually carry the behavior, yielding a graph small enough to read.

Attribution graphs are the basis of recent large-scale efforts to trace the internal reasoning of production language models, which have produced concrete case studies: features arranged into multi-hop chains for factual recall, evidence of forward planning in tasks like writing rhyming poetry, and graphs showing a model computing an answer by one route while reporting another. The approach inherits both the strengths and the caveats of the feature decomposition it rests on. It is only as faithful as the underlying sparse-autoencoder features and the linear attribution approximation, both of which can miss or distort genuine computation, and validating a graph still requires confirming its claims with direct intervention. Even with those limits, the attribution graph represents the current frontier of turning a model's behavior on a real input into a circuit a human can follow.
