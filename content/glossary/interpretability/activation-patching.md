Activation patching is the workhorse method for establishing causation inside a neural network. The procedure runs the model twice on two carefully chosen inputs, a clean run and a corrupted run that differ in one fact of interest, and then surgically copies a single internal activation from one run into the other at a specific location, leaving everything else as it was. By watching how much the final output moves when that one activation is transplanted, the experimenter learns how much that location was responsible for carrying the information. If patching a particular attention head's output restores the clean answer inside an otherwise corrupted run, that head is causally implicated in computing the answer. The test is interventional, not observational, which is what gives it force.

The method exists because correlation is not enough for mechanistic interpretability. Descriptive tools such as the logit-lens or a probing-classifier can show that some information is present at a given layer, but presence does not prove use: a representation can be readable without being read by the rest of the network. Activation patching closes that gap by intervening on the computation and observing the consequence, which is the standard that the field demands before it will say a component implements a behavior. In this sense patching is to internal components what a knockout experiment is to a gene, it severs or supplies a candidate cause and checks whether the effect follows, and it is the evidentiary backbone of nearly every circuit-discovery result.

In practice the discipline is in the experimental design, especially the choice of the corruption. The clean and corrupted inputs should differ only in the variable being studied, so that any restored behavior can be attributed to that variable and not to a confound, which is why minimal pairs are used: two prompts identical except for the name, the number, or the fact whose flow is being traced. The patch can be applied at many granularities, a whole layer, a single attention head, an individual position in the residual stream, a single feature direction, and sweeping the patch across all such sites produces a map of where the relevant information lives and when it moves. The subtlety is that the direction of patching matters: noising patches a corrupted activation into a clean run to find what is necessary, while denoising patches a clean activation into a corrupted run to find what is sufficient, and the two answers together pin down a component's role.

Activation patching is the engine behind several landmark interpretability findings and connects tightly to the rest of the toolkit. It is how the induction-head circuit and the indirect-object-identification circuit were localized, by patching head outputs and watching the answer move. Causal tracing, the variant that locates where a factual association is stored in a model so it can later be edited, is activation patching applied to memory. Patching also pairs naturally with the sparse-autoencoder: once activations are decomposed into monosemantic features, patching individual features tests their causal role one concept at a time, and it shares its interventional logic with activation-steering, which adds a direction to change behavior where patching transplants an activation to explain it. The method is not free of pitfalls, since results can depend on the corruption chosen and a single patch can miss effects distributed across several components that only matter in combination, but it remains the most direct way to turn a hypothesis about how a model computes into a claim that survives cutting the wire.
