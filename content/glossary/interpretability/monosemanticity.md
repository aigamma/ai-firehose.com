Monosemanticity is the property of representing a single meaning. A monosemantic unit, whether a neuron or a learned feature, activates for one human-recognizable concept and only that concept: a feature that fires on mentions of the Golden Gate Bridge and nothing unrelated, a feature for legal language, a feature for a specific syntactic construction. It is the direct opposite of polysemanticity, where one unit responds to a grab-bag of unrelated things. Monosemanticity is less an empirical fact about raw networks, whose neurons are stubbornly polysemantic, than the goal that interpretability methods are built to reach: a decomposition of the model into parts each of which means one thing.

The property matters because interpretability becomes tractable only when its units are monosemantic. If every feature carries exactly one concept, then understanding a representation reduces to naming its active features, and building a circuit account reduces to wiring together components whose meanings are already known. This is the dream that polysemanticity destroyed at the level of neurons and that monosemanticity restores at the level of features; the whole research program of moving from neurons to features is, in one sentence, the search for a basis in which the model's activations are monosemantic. A faithful, monosemantic decomposition is what would let a person audit a model for a dangerous capability or a deceptive feature by inspection, which is the long-run safety payoff.

The reason monosemanticity is hard to obtain, and the reason raw neurons lack it, is superposition. A network needs to represent more distinct features than it has neurons, so it packs them as overlapping near-orthogonal directions in activation space; any single neuron then sits at the crossing of several such directions and inherits a piece of each, becoming polysemantic. The route to monosemanticity is therefore to change the basis: recover the underlying directions the network actually uses, rather than reading the coordinate axes it happens to be written in. Along the right directions the features separate cleanly, even though along the neuron axes they are hopelessly entangled, which is exactly the prediction the linear-representation-hypothesis makes.

The dominant tool for recovering monosemantic features is the sparse-autoencoder, which learns an overcomplete dictionary of directions under a sparsity penalty so that each learned feature tends to fire for a single concept. Landmark work used this approach to extract large dictionaries of features from production models that are far more monosemantic than the underlying neurons, including features that track concrete entities and safety-relevant behaviors, and a named feature's meaning can then be confirmed by feature-visualization and its causal role tested by clamping it. Monosemanticity in practice is a matter of degree rather than a clean binary: dictionaries still split one concept across several features or merge several into one, and there is no guarantee the recovered features are the ones the network privately uses. Even so, maximizing monosemanticity, getting as close as possible to one feature per concept, is the explicit objective that organizes modern mechanistic interpretability.
