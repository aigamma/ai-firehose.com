An induction head is a specific, well-characterized circuit inside a transformer that implements a simple but powerful rule: if the current token appeared earlier in the context, predict that whatever came after it last time will come again. Faced with a sequence that began with the pattern "A B" and now sees "A" again, the circuit completes it with "B". This is in-context copying, and despite its simplicity it is one of the most consequential mechanisms found in language models, because the same machinery generalizes from literal repetition to fuzzy, abstract pattern continuation across the whole context window.

Induction heads matter because they are the best mechanistic explanation we have for in-context learning, the ability of a model to pick up a task from examples in its prompt without any weight updates. Their formation is tied to a sharp, measurable event during training: a sudden drop in loss accompanied by a jump in the model's ability to use long-range context, a phase change that appears across model scales. Tracing few-shot behavior back to a concrete two-head circuit was a landmark result for mechanistic interpretability, because it showed that a high-level, almost cognitive capability could be grounded in a particular arrangement of attention heads rather than remaining an emergent mystery.

The mechanism is a composition of two attention heads working across consecutive layers, which is why a single head cannot do it, the keeper. A head in the earlier layer is a previous-token head: at each position it attends one step back and copies information about the preceding token forward, so that every position now carries a trace of what came before it. The head in the later layer uses that trace to do the real work: it attends from the current token to earlier positions whose stored previous-token matches the current token, locates where this token occurred before, and reads off the token that followed, writing it toward the output. This cross-layer cooperation, communicated through the residual stream, is the canonical example of attention-head composition and the textbook illustration of what a circuit is.

Induction heads connect to several threads in interpretability. They are the headline case study for circuit analysis and a frequent target of the logit lens, which can show the copied prediction forming in the intermediate layers. Their abrupt appearance during training links them to the broader study of phase transitions and to grokking, where capabilities likewise crystallize suddenly as clean circuits replace diffuse behavior. And because their two-head structure was recovered despite the surrounding superposition, they stand as proof that real, nameable algorithms can be extracted from the dense tangle of a working model.
