---

Every generative model needs a way to score how wrong its output is, and almost all of them use a fixed, hand-designed loss: pixel error, a likelihood, a reconstruction term. A generative adversarial network throws that away and learns the loss instead. It runs two networks against each other. The generator turns a vector of random noise into a sample, an image, an audio clip, a row of data; the discriminator looks at a mix of real and generated samples and tries to tell which is which. The generator's loss is simply how well it fools the discriminator, so the thing it is judged against is not a fixed formula but a second network that is itself learning, in lockstep, to judge better.

That one move is the whole reason GANs mattered. For several years after their 2014 introduction they produced the sharpest, most photorealistic images of any method, and the learned loss is why. A variational autoencoder optimizes a pixel-wise reconstruction term, which averages over the many plausible details an output could have and so blurs them; a GAN is never told to match pixels, only to be indistinguishable from real data to a critic that has itself learned what real data looks like, so it is rewarded for committing to crisp, convincing texture rather than hedging. The StyleGAN family pushed this to faces no person could reliably call fake.

The same learned loss is the source of the GAN's notorious fragility. Because the two networks optimize opposing objectives, training is not the descent of a single loss toward a minimum but a two-player game hunting for an equilibrium, and that equilibrium is easy to miss. If the discriminator gets too good too fast, it offers the generator no usable gradient and learning stalls; if the generator finds a few outputs that reliably fool the discriminator, it may emit only those and abandon the rest of the data's variety, the failure called mode collapse. Optimizing against a moving target is far harder than descending a fixed surface, and much of GAN research is about stabilizing this game, the Wasserstein GAN's smoother objective being the best-known example.

Placed beside the other generative families, the GAN is the one that refuses to compute a probability at all. An autoregressive model and a normalizing flow give exact likelihoods; a variational autoencoder gives a bound; a GAN gives neither, learning the data distribution only implicitly through the adversarial signal, exactly the trade that buys sample sharpness at the price of a tractable density and stable training. For high-fidelity image synthesis the diffusion model has now largely overtaken it, reaching comparable or better quality through a single stable regression objective rather than a game. What keeps GANs alive is speed: a GAN makes a sample in one forward pass where diffusion needs many, so wherever generation must be instant the adversarial generator still earns its keep.
