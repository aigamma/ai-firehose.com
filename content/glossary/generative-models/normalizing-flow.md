A normalizing flow models a complex data distribution by warping a simple one through an exactly invertible transformation. You start with an easy distribution you can sample and evaluate, typically a standard Gaussian, and pass its samples through a sequence of bijective functions whose net effect bends the simple shape into the shape of the data. Because the transformation is invertible and its components are designed so the change of variables can be computed, the model supports both directions: push a data point backward to the simple space to read off its exact likelihood, or sample from the simple space and push forward to generate new data.

Normalizing flows matter because they are the generative family that delivers exact likelihoods together with direct, single-pass sampling. A variational autoencoder only bounds the likelihood, and a generative adversarial network provides no likelihood at all, but a flow computes the precise probability it assigns to any data point, which makes flows attractive wherever an exact density is the goal: anomaly detection, density estimation, modeling complex priors and posteriors inside other models, and scientific applications where a calibrated likelihood matters as much as a realistic sample.

The exactness comes from the change-of-variables formula, which says that when you transform a random variable through an invertible function, its density is rescaled by the Jacobian determinant of that function. Evaluating a likelihood therefore requires computing this determinant at every layer, and a general determinant is far too expensive in high dimensions, so the art of flow design is constructing transformations that are expressive yet have a cheaply computable Jacobian determinant, for example coupling layers that update one half of the variables as a function of the other half, which makes the Jacobian triangular and its determinant a simple product. Stacking many such layers builds the expressive power needed to match real data while keeping every step tractable and invertible.

The architectural constraint of exact invertibility is the flow's defining trade-off, and it situates the model among its relatives. Forcing every layer to be invertible with a tractable determinant limits expressiveness per parameter, so flows have generally not matched the raw sample quality of a generative adversarial network or a diffusion model on images. This constraint is precisely what the closely named flow matching framework relaxes: it learns the continuous velocity field that defines a flow by regression rather than building the network from exactly invertible blocks, trading the exact-likelihood guarantee for the scale and quality needed in modern high-resolution generation. Flows also connect to the autoregressive model, since an autoregressive transformation is itself an invertible map with a triangular Jacobian, and several flow architectures are built directly on that observation.
