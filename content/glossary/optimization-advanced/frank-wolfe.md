The Frank-Wolfe algorithm, also called the conditional gradient method, minimizes a smooth convex function over a convex constraint set without the projection step that most constrained optimizers depend on. Projected gradient descent handles constraints by taking an unconstrained step and snapping the result back onto the feasible set, but that projection can itself be an expensive optimization for a complicated set. Frank-Wolfe avoids it entirely: at each iteration it linearizes the objective at the current point using the gradient, minimizes that linear function over the feasible set, a linear program whose solution is always a vertex or extreme point, and moves a fraction of the way toward that extreme point.

Because every iterate is a convex combination of extreme points it has visited, Frank-Wolfe stays feasible automatically and, crucially, structurally simple. Over many constraint sets of interest the linear subproblem is far cheaper than a projection: minimizing a linear function over the unit ball of a norm, a probability simplex, or the set of low-trace-norm matrices often has a closed form or a cheap sparse solution. The pull toward an extreme point also means the iterate is built from a small number of them, so the method naturally produces sparse or low-rank solutions, the extreme points being sparse vectors or rank-one matrices, and that implicit structure is a major reason it is used for sparse and low-rank machine learning.

It is best understood as the natural counterpart to proximal and projected methods within constrained convex optimization. Where proximal gradient and mirror descent reckon with a constraint or a nonsmooth penalty through a proximal or projection operator, Frank-Wolfe reckons with it through a linear minimization oracle, and which is cheaper depends entirely on the geometry of the set; for the structured sets common in statistics and signal processing the linear oracle wins decisively. Its step size is typically a simple decaying schedule or a line search, and it comes with a clean convergence guarantee, error shrinking at a rate of one over the iteration count for smooth convex objectives, plus a computable duality gap that gives an honest stopping certificate at every step.

Its limitations mark where it is not the right tool. Its convergence rate is slower than the accelerated first-order methods, and on problems whose solution lies on the boundary it can zigzag, a behavior remedied by variants that add away-steps. But for large-scale constrained problems where projection is the bottleneck and a linear oracle is cheap, it is often the most practical choice, and its projection-free, sparsity-inducing character keeps it relevant in modern large-scale convex optimization.
