In an evolutionary strategy, selection plays the role the gradient plays in gradient descent. Rather than tracking a single point and stepping along its derivative, the method maintains a distribution over candidate solutions, usually a Gaussian, and improves it generation by generation: it samples a population of candidates, evaluates the objective on each, and shifts the distribution toward the ones that scored best, repeating until the population converges on a good region. The direction of improvement is inferred from ranked function values rather than computed from derivatives, which is the whole point.

Each generation runs a sample-evaluate-update loop. New candidates are drawn by perturbing the current mean with random noise, the best performers are kept and the rest discarded, and their positions move the mean and, in the more sophisticated variants, reshape the covariance that controls the spread and orientation of future samples. The leading method, CMA-ES (Covariance Matrix Adaptation), adapts that full covariance so the search ellipsoid stretches along productive directions and shrinks across unproductive ones, automatically learning the local scale and correlation structure of the landscape, a curvature-aware character that, strikingly, turns out to be closely related to a natural gradient step taken on the parameters of the sampling distribution.

Evolutionary strategies earn their place precisely where gradients are unavailable, unreliable, or unhelpful. They need only a black-box objective value, so they handle non-differentiable, discontinuous, or noisy functions and objectives reached only through a simulator with no analytic form; because evaluations are independent they parallelize almost perfectly across machines; and because selection depends only on the rank of the scores, not their magnitudes, they shrug off noise and badly scaled or deceptive objectives. They also resist getting stuck, since a spread-out population explores several basins at once, unlike gradient descent, which commits to whichever basin it started in.

This robustness costs sample efficiency, because estimating progress from a population of evaluations needs many more objective queries than reading one exact gradient, which is why networks trained on differentiable losses still use backpropagation. But where the objective is a genuine black box, tuning a controller, designing through a physics simulator, or optimizing the non-differentiable reward of a reinforcement-learning policy, evolutionary strategies are a serious, scalable alternative, sitting in the derivative-free family beside simulated annealing and Bayesian optimization and trading the gradient's precision for the freedom to optimize functions a gradient method cannot touch.
