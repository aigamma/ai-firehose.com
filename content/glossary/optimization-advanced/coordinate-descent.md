Coordinate descent attacks a high-dimensional optimization problem by never solving it in full. At each step it picks one coordinate, freezes every other variable at its current value, minimizes the objective along that single axis, then moves to the next coordinate and repeats, cycling through all of them until the solution stops changing. The appeal is that a one-dimensional minimization is often easy, sometimes available in closed form, even when the full problem is not. Where gradient descent moves all parameters together along the negative gradient, coordinate descent moves along the axes one at a time, a different and sometimes far cheaper decomposition of the same descent.

The method shines when the per-coordinate update is cheap and the problem has structure that makes a full gradient step costly or awkward. The canonical example is the Lasso, the sparse linear regression that penalizes the sum of absolute coefficient values: its objective is non-differentiable wherever a coefficient is zero, which trips up plain gradient methods, yet optimizing one coefficient at a time has a clean closed form, a soft-thresholding operation that sets small coefficients exactly to zero. Cycling that update across coefficients is the standard and remarkably fast way to fit Lasso models, and it naturally produces the exact zeros that make the solution sparse. The same one-axis strategy underlies efficient solvers for many other regularized regression and support-vector problems.

Coordinate descent matters because for the right problems it is both simple and competitive with far more sophisticated methods, with almost no tuning. There is no learning rate to set when the coordinate-wise minimization is exact, the memory footprint is minimal, and each update touches only the data relevant to one variable, which is efficient on sparse designs. The order of visiting coordinates is a genuine knob: sweeping in a fixed cycle is simplest, choosing coordinates at random often converges faster and is easier to analyze, and choosing greedily the coordinate with the largest gradient component can be faster still at the cost of finding it.

There is one way it stalls, and it is instructive. For smooth convex objectives it provably converges to the global minimum, but for non-smooth functions that are not separable across coordinates it can jam at a point optimal along every single axis yet not optimal overall, stuck in a corner from which no one-coordinate move improves things even though a diagonal move would. Lasso escapes this because its non-smooth penalty is separable, a sum of per-coordinate terms, which is precisely the structure that makes coordinate descent safe. Block coordinate descent, which optimizes a small group of variables jointly at each step, mitigates the stalling on coupled problems and extends the method to settings where single-axis moves are too restrictive.
