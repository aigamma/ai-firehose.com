Mirror descent is gradient descent re-derived for problems whose natural geometry is not Euclidean. Ordinary gradient descent can be read as solving a tiny subproblem at each step: minimize a linear approximation of the loss plus a penalty for moving too far, with "too far" measured by squared Euclidean distance. That squared-distance penalty quietly assumes every direction in parameter space is interchangeable, and when the feasible region is a probability simplex, a positive cone, or some other skewed or constrained domain, that assumption fits badly and the steps are inefficient.

Mirror descent swaps the Euclidean penalty for a Bregman divergence generated by a chosen convex "mirror map" tailored to the domain, and the mechanics earn the name: the algorithm maps the current point through the gradient of the mirror map into a dual space, takes an ordinary additive gradient step there, and maps back. Choose the squared norm as the mirror map and the familiar update reappears; choose the negative entropy and you get multiplicative weight updates and the exponentiated gradient method, which are far more natural on the simplex than additive steps that would have to be projected back into it after every move.

Matching the optimizer to the geometry sharpens both convergence and constraint handling, sometimes spectacularly. On a high-dimensional simplex the entropic version of mirror descent has a convergence bound that scales only logarithmically with the dimension, where Euclidean gradient descent scales with the square root, an exponential gap in how the difficulty grows with size. It is also a foundational tool in online learning and regret minimization, underpinning much of the theory of learning from adversarial sequences.

Mirror descent sits in a tight cluster of geometry-aware methods. It is closely related to the natural gradient, which likewise replaces Euclidean steps with ones measured in a problem-specific metric, and the two coincide for certain choices of geometry; its per-step "linearize, then penalize the move" template is exactly the structure proximal gradient methods reuse to handle nonsmooth terms. Together these form the modern view of first-order optimization as gradient descent re-derived under whatever notion of distance the problem actually demands.
